## Part 1

### 1

The *August Celeste* pulled into Merranth harbour at the peak of
morning, when the light was doing its best to promise a good day, and
the gulls shitting on deck were doing their best to ruin it.

Tav stood at the bow, his hat lifted up, the sun warming his face, and
his hands in his pockets. He had been here for a while. He wanted to
watch the city come into focus. Five years does something to a skyline,
or maybe it did more to him as he gazed upon his former home with
nostalgia.

Merranth looked the same: the same sandstone warehouses stacked along
the docks, the same tangle of masts and rigging, the same smell of brine
and fish and something underneath that might have been ambition, or just
old rotting wood. He'd missed it.

*Long time, Father.*

He pulled a ring up from a necklace beneath his shirt and turned it once
between his fingers. Plain silver, faded and scratched from wear; stolen
by a girl who'd caught him off guard in a port city two years ago.

He tucked it away again as the *August Celeste* drew into her berth.

---

The harbourmaster was a short man with a pock-marked face and a weaselly
moustache. He came down to the dock to meet Tav personally, which was
unusual.

"Rare to see a single passenger on a cargo run, lad," the man said, in
the tone of someone who was curious and pretending otherwise. Last
night's ale still clung to his breath. He tucked in his unkempt shirt.
He's smuggling a large cannonball in his gut, Tav thought.

"Rarer still to see a harbourmaster stand on principle so early in the
morning," Tav said. "Lodgings?"

The harbourmaster smiled. "I know just the place. Dock Street, not a
five minutes walk. Clean enough, reasonable price, good people. But
there's the matter of undocumented entrants to Merranth." The
harbourmaster produced a rolled-up leaf of paper from his back pocket,
and a quill from behind his ear.

"Last name," he asked.

Tav noticed the man's pen hovering a little too eagerly. "Derring," he
said, watching for the reaction.

There it was. A slight straightening as he perked up.

"And you're coming from Sudlow, Mr. Derring?"

"Please, call me Vic. And yes. I'm from Merranth originally and I've
returned home."

The harbourmaster smiled and began to write. Tav produced a coin and
held it up between two fingers. The harbourmaster's eyes tracked it
immediately.

"Say, is it normal to document all people visiting or returning home? I
don't recall the custom."

"New policy, I'm afraid. We document entrants for the register."

"Mm." Tav let the coin catch the light, then pulled it back as the man
reached for it. "Ah. put me down as Vic."

The harbourmaster looked at him, and Tav smiled pleasantly.

"Vic," the man repeated, and wrote it down.

Tav flicked the coin through the air, and the harbourmaster caught it
against his chest, undignified about it.

"Welcome home, Vic. I have what I need. I'll add you to the register,"
he burped. "You'll be staying on Dock Street then, I presume? In case
there are any issues processing your entry, you see."

"Yep," he said because it sounded easier and because he was tired.
Something was off about the man's interest but he'd been at sea for
weeks and his instincts deserved one morning off.

*Sloppy,* said a small voice deep in his mind.

He told it to mind its own business.

---

The room was up two flights of stairs, facing an alley, with a narrow
bed and a window that looked like it could open. Tav dropped his pack on
the floor and his hat on the chair and stood in the middle of the room
for a moment, just breathing.

Then he sat on the edge of the bed and opened the pack.

He took them out one at a time. A copper mask from somewhere in Nemogal,
features worn smooth by handling. A carved stone crow, small enough to
sit in a closed fist. A brass disc etched with a navigator's pattern he
still hadn't decoded. A wooden figure, a sailor or merchant depending on
the angle, which he'd lifted from a governor's bureau. There were
others. Fourteen in total.

He returned them to his pack. None of them were expensive, which hadn't
been the point. His father liked collecting things that had seen or
represented the world. Objects with weight to them, that fixed a moment
in place and said *I was here, and this happened, and it mattered.*

Twenty-six ports over five years. Not bad for a kid from Merranth with a
frozen debt and a tendency to antagonize authority figures. He smoothed
a thumb across the crow's carved wing.

*I'll bring them home soon.* When everything was finished. He'd go to
the safehouse when it was time. When he'd done what he set out to
achieve. The painting. The trinkets. The things that belonged to his
father, returned to where they ought to be.

He had a perfect plan. Several details still to resolve, but the shape
of it was good. It would be perfect.

He lay back on the bed, with his boots still on, and put his hat over
his face.

He had paid the harbourmaster a gold piece; likely more than the man
would see in a month, which was probably enough. Probably. The thought
sat in his chest, small and irritating, and he couldn't quite set it
down.

He turned a ring between his fingers once in the dark beneath his shirt,
and slept.

### 2

He had heard them before he was fully awake.

Silent footfalls on the third floor above. Careful and measured. Someone
knew where to step and when. Dust sifted down from the ceiling and
caught the moonlight streaming through the gap in the curtain. Tav lay
still for two full counts, eyes open. He made a quick mental inventory.
Knives by the bed. Pack by the small table. Window.

He couldn't take his pack. He tipped his hat back, tucked the silver
ring back under his shirt, crossed to the table in two strides and slid
the pack flat across the floor, under the bed. It would have to do. He
moved to the window and pushed.

Stiff from disuse. Three hits. The window had opinions about being woken
at this hour.

He was out onto the sill and up the drainpipe. He hung for a second,
cold iron under his hands. The city spread out below him, grey and
silver in the wee small hours, and the harbour beyond it catching the
moon.

*Sloppy*, said the voice from yesterday.

He told it he was busy.

---

His pursuers were wrong. That was the first thing he noted once he had
roof under his feet and momentum carrying him forward across the slick
tiles. Not slow, not clumsy, nothing so useful. They anticipated him.
They took the gaps he took without breaking stride, silent,
synchronized, patient as arithmetic, and no crew of the watch and no
rival\'s hired knives had ever moved like that.

He cut left across a flat stretch of tile, took the gap between two
buildings at a run, caught the opposite ledge with both hands and swung
himself up.

He checked behind himself. Two shapes against the skyline, already
closing.

*Shadows. His next stride came down short, and the cold of the roof
tiles arrived through his boots all at once, as if it had been waiting
for permission.*

He ran.

Merranth opened up beneath him, and he played the still familiar terrain
like an accordion: jutting chimneys, dilapidated shingles, canopied
roof. He'd grown up on these rooftops. From a glance, he could tell
which ledges could hold his weight, and which would introduce him to the
street below. He knew the gap above Chandler's yard was jumpable if you
hit it from the right angle, knew the old aqueduct straddling the
Warrens gave you ten feet of clear running if he didn't think too hard
about the drop on either side. So, he did exactly that: he stopped
thinking and let the city call to him.

Left off the aqueduct. Down a drainpipe to the alley below, boots
hitting wet cobble, he landed into a passage barely wide enough for his
shoulders. Stone scraped his arms as he pushed through. He emerged into
a small square, dark windows, a cat that vanished without commentary. He
didn't slow down.

*I've lost them*, he thought, which was either true or optimistic and
there wasn't much way to tell at this speed.

He rounded the corner into a wider alley and took his first deep breath
since the start of the pursuit.

---

A large man filled the far end of the alley. Tav had approximately one
second to register the size of him, the pale complexion almost luminous
in the dark, the shock of red hair, the gnarled truncheon resting easy
against one shoulder.

One second was not enough time to stop.

He managed, "Sloppy..." before the truncheon took a shortcut to his
temple. He appreciated the alley turning sideways before it went away
entirely.

### 3

The view from Sayd's office was the best thing about Lowertown, which
wasn't saying much for Lowertown.

He stood at the window, his pipe burning slow between two fingers,
waiting to watch the harbour catch the early light. Merranth from above
looked almost orderly: the geometry of rooftops, the neat lines of the
docks, the ships at anchor sitting still as painted things. The illusion
lasted until you descended into it, which Sayd tried to do as
infrequently as possible these days.

He'd been at the window for a bell. He'd known Tav was in the city for
two.

---

The door opened without a knock. Sayd didn't turn around.

"He's on Dock Street," Odd Hands said, slightly out of breath from the
stairs. "Lodging house, third floor. The harbourmaster confirmed it."

"I know."

A pause. "You... right. Yes. Should I..."

"The Shadows are already moving," Sayd drew on the tabac and exhaled
slowly. "Close the door."

The door closed. Sayd listened to Odd Hands. The particular quality of
silence that meant someone was hovering, working up a question.

"He'll run for height," Odd Hands said, finally. "If they push him from
the lodging house."

"And?"

"The rooftops off Dock Street lead toward the Warrens."

"They do."

Another pause. Sayd could feel the boy calculating. He had a good mind,
though raw and unstructured, working too fast and occasionally in the
wrong direction. It would be something in time.

"You want them to push him toward the Warrens," Odd Hands said, "before
he can get lost in the city."

Sayd turned from the window.

"Send word to Halst," he said. "The derelict boarding house on Cutter's.
Tav likes rooftops, so he'll run for height and work his way inward.
Push him east off the aqueduct and he'll come down to the street level
somewhere between Chandler's yard and the harbour gate." He drew on the
tabac again. "Halst knows the rest."

Odd Hands was already moving.

"Then you check his room. He wouldn't have had time to take everything.
Have his belongings collected and bring them to Judgment Crane."

"What do we do with them?"

Sayd turned back to the window. Down at the painting-like stillness of
the harbour. *Always trust a harbourmaster, eh Tav.*

"You're going to perform for our guest," he said.

Sayd waited. He had made a study of waiting, waiting for the pieces to
fall into place and letting the second move be made. Patience was a
currency most didn't know how to appreciate. Tav had never been good at
waiting. That clearly hadn't changed; the harbourmaster's account of
their exchange had made that clear enough.

The tabac burned down to nothing. Sayd didn't reach for another.

*You reckon you escaped the Accord, Tav? Nobody escapes.*

He turned back to the door and left to make a long overdue
re-acquaintance.

### 4

He came back to the world in bad order.

His skull first. A deep wrongness behind his left ear where the
truncheon had landed, a warmth wrapping his brain that he could swear
pulsed audibly. Then his wrists, bound behind him, and his fingers
already working at the knots before the rest of him had caught up.
Coarse weave over his face. Hood. His own breath coming back hot against
his mouth, tasting of iron. He tongued the split in his lip and kept his
head loose on his neck.

Somewhere in front of him, a man was weeping.

The sound cut through the fog before the rest of it. Real weeping, the
kind that costs something. Then a voice, low, conversational, almost
friendly: \"You understand that I take no pleasure in this.\"

*Where---*

The hood came off.

Too much light. Tav squinted through it, eyes streaming. A cellar,
low-beamed, torches set along the walls. Slowly: a table. On the table,
a young man, stripped to the waist and lashed flat. And standing over
him, running a match slowly between two fingers, was a tall thin shape
that looked like someone had built a man out of angles and spite.
Long-limbed, narrow, a face that had seen everything at least once and
remembered the details. He moved with an odd hitch, one leg trailing,
and nothing about him suggested threat until you noticed the boy on the
table couldn\'t look away from his hands.

*Inquisitor*, Tav thought, and his stomach made its opinion known.

The thin man struck the match. Held it between them. The boy sobbed
something that wanted to be a name and didn\'t quite get there, and
Tav\'s hands worked harder at the knots, fingers clumsy and thick from
the blow. Nothing gave. Whoever had done the binding knew their work.

Then the match-flame leaned.

Not in a draft. There was no draft. The flame bent a finger\'s width
toward the boy and held there, and something cold passed over the room,
settling on Tav\'s skin like damp cloth. A half-second, maybe less. Then
the match burned down to the thin man\'s fingers and he dropped it
without flinching.

Tav\'s head throbbed. He blinked. The cold was already gone.
Showmanship, he decided. The thin one had a trick with the matches.
Inquisitors always had a trick.

His hands had stopped working at the rope. He made them start again.

*Think.* Hood, chair, bound wrists, an inquisitor performing for someone
who isn\'t the boy. Torches set for the room\'s benefit, not the
work\'s. No tools on the table. No blood. The boy was terrified, but the
terror was too obvious; the boy's skin was unmarked, untouched.

*Ah.*

So it was theatre. He was the audience. That helped, not as much as he
would have liked, but enough to get his breathing under control and his
mouth into the shape of a grin before anyone noticed it had been
elsewhere.

That was also when he saw the shelf.

His trinkets. All fourteen, set out in a neat row at a precise and
deliberate distance. The copper mask and the carved stone crow and the
brass disc he still hadn\'t decoded, arranged by someone with a clerk\'s
love of order. He\'d slid his pack under the bed and gone out the window
and thought himself clever for it. Seeing them out here, handled,
*inventoried*, was worse than the rope.

He became aware of the necklace against his sternum, the plain silver
ring sitting warm under his shirt where a careful man would have looked
and a contemptuous one hadn\'t bothered. Small mercy. He\'d take it.

And under it, lower, the old mark across his chest sat warm too. Warmer
than the room warranted. He shifted against the rope. Sea air. Gets into
the skin after a while.

Somewhere behind the light, a slow clap began.

Three beats, unhurried. The thin inquisitor stepped back into the
shadows without a word, and the boy on the table stopped weeping. Just
stopped, like a tap shut off. He sat up, rubbing his wrists, and had the
grace to look a little embarrassed about the whole thing.

The scent reached Tav before the man did. Ginger, warm and dry. He knew
that smell the way you know a voice through a wall.

\"You can stop performing, Odd Hands.\" Sayd stepped into the light.
\"He\'s awake, and he\'s stopped being frightened, which means the
educational portion is over.\"

He looked the same. That was what Tav noticed first and trusted least.
Refined, unhurried, dressed in dark good cloth that belonged in no
particular city, a pipe burning slow between two fingers. He looked at
Tav the way a man looks at a sum.

\"Sayd.\" Tav\'s voice came out rough, so he leaned into it. \"You\'ve
redecorated. The weeping boy is new. Very evocative. A bit much for a
reunion, but you always did enjoy the presentation.\"

\"And you always arrive unprepared for it.\"

\"How long has it been?\"

\"Five years, two months.\" Sayd drew on the pipe.

Tav looked past him to the gentleman with the truncheon, who stood by
the door, filling most of it. Vast and Palor pale, red hair gone faded
at the sides, the gnarled club resting easy against one shoulder. Halst
watched everything and contributed nothing, which seemed to suit him
fine.

Tav tipped his chin at him. \"No hard feelings, big man. You were only
doing your job.\" He grinned and felt it pull at the split on his lip.
\"Pull the next one, though, would you? I\'m a good actor. I\'d have
gone down lovely for you with half the swing.\"

Halst looked at him for a moment. \"You went down lovely anyway,\" he
said, and that was all he had to say about it.

Tav decided he liked Halst. He also decided to keep testing the rope
behind his back while he said so. Neither decision needed to interfere
with the other.

Sayd pulled a chair around and sat, and Tav noted it, because Sayd did
nothing without choosing to. Sitting put them at the same height. Like
old friends reuniting with one of them tied to a chair, beaten and
bruised.

\"Your debt isn\'t gone,\" Sayd said. \"You know that. You\'ve always
known that. You did something clever five years ago, and the whole of
the Accord decided to pretend it didn't happen. That isn't the same as
gone.\" He let the pipe smoke thin between them.

The mark on Tav\'s chest felt warmer. A slow itch under the skin. He
shifted against it and kept his face pleasant.

\"You\'d buy my debt. Out of sentiment.\"

\"Out of curiosity.\" Sayd\'s mouth did something that wasn\'t quite a
smile. \"I want to know how you did it. Nobody walks out, Tav. You
walked out. I have spent five years wanting to know how, and you are
going to tell me, and in exchange I will make you a free man in the only
sense that matters to people like us.\"

\"And if I\'d rather keep my secrets and my headache?\"

\"Then you keep your debt. And the Accord keeps its patience, which I\'m
sure is a great comfort. They\'ve been patient for five years now.
Generous of them.\" Sayd tapped the pipe against his thumb. \"I wonder
how long generosity lasts when it isn\'t costing them anything to
stop.\"

\"I want my things back.\"

\"They aren\'t your things.\" Sayd said it gently, which was the worst
way he could have said it. \"You left them under a bed and fled out a
window. *That which has been abandoned is for someone new to claim.*\"
He turned the old words over without weight, the way you\'d quote
scripture. \"You remember the lesson. They taught it to us both.\"

The line sat in the cold air.

\"They\'re mine until you\'ve earned them,\" Sayd went on. \"There are
building plans I need that you\'re well placed to acquire. Bring me
those, and the items on the shelf are yours again, every one of them.\"
He nodded toward the boy, Odd Hands, who had pulled his shirt back on
and was watching the exchange with naked interest. \"And you take the
boy. He\'s mine, after a fashion. Gifted. Untaught. I\'d like him to see
a professional work, so he learns the parts I can\'t show him.\"

\"Shadow work.\" Tav worked his wrists against the binding. Not hiding
it now. \"Babysitting your apprentice on a live job.\"

\"Call it what you like.\"

\"I call it a service. Services have prices.\" He pulled at the rope,
casual, like a man testing a hammock. \"I\'ll take the boy. He can carry
things. But it costs you, and it happens I\'ve got a list.\"

Sayd waited. He had always been good at waiting. Tav had forgotten how
much room the man left in a conversation, how the silence sat there
daring you to fill it. He\'d forgotten, too, that filling it was usually
a mistake, and he filled it anyway because that was another thing about
Tav. One that he hadn't bothered to fix.

\"Pendleton.\" He watched Sayd\'s face the way he\'d watched the
harbourmaster\'s pen. \"The Royal Procession stops there. I\'m going to
take it. Not the procession, the deposits, the whole vault of it while
the crown\'s looking the other way. I\'ve got two problems. I need an
inside man with a reason to be inside, and I need to know the room
before I\'m standing in it. You can solve both.\"

He\'d watched for the reaction. There wasn\'t one.

\"I can solve both,\" Sayd agreed.

\"Inspector\'s credentials. The advance kind, the ones that let a man
sweep a vault for faults before the important guests arrive. Genuine
enough to survive a careful look.\" Tav had a face in mind as he said
it, a broad warm face with patched elbows, a man who could stand in any
room and look like he was supposed to be there. \"And whatever you can
get me on what\'s behind those walls.\"

\"The credentials, yes. The intelligence, within reason.\" Sayd tapped
ash onto the stone floor. \"You steal the plans. The boy shadows the
job. Your shelf comes home. I provide what you need for Pendleton, and
the boy goes through the doors with you when the day comes, because I
want eyes I trust on a thing this size.\" He stood, and the conversation
was apparently over because Sayd had decided it was. \"And after
Pendleton, you tell me how you froze the debt.\"

It wasn\'t shaped like a question.

Tav looked up at him. The man he\'d shared a cell-block education with,
traded bread with, trusted in the specific narrow way you trust someone
who is surviving the same thing as you. There was a warmth there still,
underneath. He was almost sure of it.

\"After Pendleton,\" Tav said. \"And you tell me how you got out
yourself. Don\'t pretend you didn\'t. You\'re standing here in good
cloth with a boy of your own, an inquisitor, and a man like Halst at the
door.\"

For a moment Sayd just looked at him. Then: \"It\'s a deal.\"

He nodded to Halst, and Halst came to cut the rope, and the whole
performance was over, and they were friends again, in whatever sense the
word still applied to the two of them.

It was only later, rubbing the feeling back into his wrists in the cold
evening air, his stolen trinkets behind him and the boy trailing a
careful ten paces back, that the thing settled which had been bothering
him the whole time. Sayd had bought bread for him once, when neither of
them had any. Given it, not traded it. Tav had spent the whole
conversation waiting for a flicker of that, and it hadn\'t come.

Everything had a price. Even the friendship had come itemized.

*Sloppy*, said the voice, about something else entirely.

## Part 2

### 1

The surveyor\'s office sat above a chandler\'s shop on Salt Street, and
the lock on the second-floor door existed to reassure the man who owned
it. Tav had picked its cousins a hundred times and its betters more than
once.

He studied the building anyway, chin on his fists on the roof opposite,
working through it the way his father had taught him. Where the light
fell. Where the stairs would speak. Where a man would stand if he wanted
to be unseen, and where he\'d stand if he only believed he was. The rear
stair climbed the back of the building to a single door, and the front
window looked out over the street, and the whole block smelled of
tallow, a fatty sweetness that crept down the throat and set up
residence.

Beside him, Odd Hands\' fingers were drumming against his knees.

\"Stop that.\"

\"Stop what?\"

\"The tapping. You\'re introducing yourself to every tile on this
roof.\"

The fingers stilled. Tav gave it four counts. On the fifth they started
again, softer, which the boy presumably considered a compromise.

The job was simple, and Tav had learned a healthy suspicion of simple.
The surveyor held floor plans for half the merchant quarter, filed by
client name, sealed in wax that would announce any curious fingers to
anyone who cared to look. Sayd wanted one set. Tav hadn\'t asked whose,
or why. Questions had a price with Sayd, same as everything else, and he
was already carrying more of the man\'s ledger than he liked the weight
of.

\"Stay on this roof,\" he said. \"Watch the street. Someone comes to the
front, you tap the window frame twice. The frame, not the glass.\"

\"And if someone comes from the back?\"

\"Then you\'ve learned something useful about the building.\"

He went down the far side, crossed low, and took the rear stair with his
weight on the outer edges of the treads, where old wood complains least.
The door lock took eleven counts, which was eight more than it deserved.
His hands were still tender from the rope. He let himself in, eased the
door to behind him, and turned the lock again, because a locked door was
the cheapest lie in the business and he never passed up a bargain.

A small office. Desk, cabinet, a cot in the corner that told a story
about the surveyor\'s marriage, rolled parchment standing in a rack like
organ pipes. Moonlight through the front window laid a pale grid across
the floorboards. The wax-sealed plans lived in a chest behind the desk,
and the chest wore a lock with genuine ambitions.

Tav knelt and introduced himself to it.

It was good work. Better than the door deserved to have guarding
anything, which meant the surveyor knew exactly which of his papers were
worth stealing. Tav\'s fingers found the mechanism and started the
conversation, patient, each pin a question with one right answer and a
hundred wrong ones. The first pin gave. The second was shy.

Two taps sounded on the window frame.

He went still, hands still in the lock. Correct signal, correctly given.
He breathed out through his nose and listened past his own pulse. Boots
on cobble, coming up the street. Uneven. A man walking with the
concentrated dignity of the very drunk. Fine. Drunks passed. He waited
for the boots to fade.

Then a voice floated up from the street, young, pitched beautifully to
carry.

\"Spare a coin, master? Haven\'t eaten since. Since yesterday.\"

Tav closed his eyes for the length of one specific thought.

That was not the signal. That was not any signal known to the trade.
That was his lookout, down off the roof and standing in the middle of
Salt Street, performing hunger at a drunk.

The boots had stopped. Keys jangled, close, the small brass argument of
a man hunting the right one. Not passing through, then. Coming home.
Sayd\'s notes had called the surveyor regular in his habits, home by
dark and church-sober, and Tav filed that away in the drawer he kept for
things Sayd had been wrong about, which until tonight had been empty.

He put his attention back in his fingers. The chest first. If he left
now, he left with nothing and the door relocked and the whole night
spent for the privilege of doing it again. Two more pins. He worked them
while the voices negotiated below, the boy\'s bright and pleading, the
man\'s thick and unkind.

\"Off my step.\"

\"Your step, master? Thought this was the chandler\'s.\"

\"It\'s over the chandler\'s, and it\'s mine, and you\'re on the step of
it. Off.\"

Third pin. The boy shifted tactics, and Tav heard it happen, heard the
pivot in his voice, and in the middle of everything felt a flicker of
professional appreciation he\'d examine later, if there was a later.

\"Only I think you dropped your purse, master. Back there by the trough.
Heard it go.\"

A pause. The keys stopped. Greed and suspicion held a brief committee
meeting in the dark, and greed lost.

\"I know a dip when I see one, boy. Off, before I whistle for the
watch.\"

The watch. Grand. The fourth pin surrendered and the bolt drew back and
Tav had the chest open, moonlight on wax seals, a clerk\'s neat hand
naming names. He found the set he\'d been sent for, lifted it whole and
unbroken, and slid it flat into the long pocket of his coat. He closed
the chest, and then, because a job worth doing was worth denying, he
relocked it. Boots on the rear stair now, each tread announcing the man
like a herald with a grudge.

Front window, then. Over the street, where a neighbor\'s shutter had
already come open at the noise and lamplight was leaning out of it. Or
he stayed, in a room the size of a confession booth, with a drunk
between him and the only door.

He stayed.

He crossed to the corner past the cot, into the black the moon grid
didn\'t reach, and folded himself against the wall the way you fold a
coat you intend to wear again. The key scratched the lock. Scratched it
again. The surveyor was having a difficult evening with doors. On the
third attempt it turned, and the man shouldered in and stood swaying in
the doorway, breathing ale into his own office.

He didn\'t light the lamp. Small mercies from small gods. He aimed
himself at the cot with the deliberation of a ship coming in to berth,
and Tav moved when he moved, one man\'s stagger covering the other
man\'s steps, crossed the moon grid in the noise of the surveyor\'s
boots hitting the floorboards one after the other, and was at the front
window with his fingers under the sash when the cot took its cargo with
a groan of ropes.

The window came up sweet and silent. Oiled. The one thing in the
building the man maintained. Tav gave him a nod he\'d never see and went
out onto the sill.

The drainpipe was an arm\'s reach to the left, and there was already
someone on it.

Odd Hands, coming up. They looked at each other, thief and apprentice,
one descending a building and one climbing it, and there was a moment of
perfect shared understanding in which each of them recognized the other
as the stupidest person he had ever met. Then the bracket under the
boy\'s foot tore out of the brick.

It didn\'t go quietly. Iron shrieked on stone, and the pipe swung out
from the wall with the boy riding it like a mast in a gale, and Tav
leaned out from the sill and caught a fistful of collar and hauled
sideways. For one count they hung there, both of them, redistributing
their opinions about gravity. Then the boy\'s boots found the sill and
Tav\'s shoulder found the window frame and the pipe finished its
argument with the wall and clattered its top length into the street.

The neighbor\'s lamp swung toward the sound. Light climbed the front of
the building.

\"Roof,\" Tav breathed, and they went up instead of down, over the
gutter and onto the tiles, and the lamplight caught them going. It
caught them clean. Tav felt it on his back the way you feel a hand
there, and below, a woman\'s voice was already assembling a description
for the whole street\'s benefit, night clothes and outrage: a boy, and a
tall fellow in a dark coat, blue at the collar, there, on the roof,
THERE.

They crossed two rooftops, dropped into the alley behind the cooper\'s,
and ran the honest way, on the ground, until Salt Street was a smell
behind them.

---

The alley they stopped in was narrow enough to touch both walls, and Tav
did, one hand on each, head down, letting his breathing come back to
him. Odd Hands stood three paces off with his back against the brick.
Ready to move. Always ready to move, even now, even nowhere.

\"The street was clear when I signaled,\" the boy said. \"Then he came
around the corner and he had keys, and you were still inside, so I.\"

\"So you improvised.\"

\"I stalled him. It worked. You got the plans, didn\'t you?\"

Tav straightened. He took a moment choosing what to do with his voice,
because the first three things it wanted to do were no use to anyone.

\"I got the plans. And a woman on Salt Street got my coat, and my
height, and your face, and the watch will have all of it by the time
that man sobers up enough to count his papers.\" He tapped the long
pocket. \"The seals are clean. He may never count them. That\'s the
thread the whole night is hanging by, and you cut it climbing a
drainpipe I was already standing on.\"

\"You were trapped.\"

\"I was working. There\'s a difference, and the difference is the job.\"
He crouched to the boy\'s level, which cost his knees something after
the rooftops. \"You signaled right. Frame, twice, textbook. And then you
stopped trusting the man you\'d signaled to. Everything after that, the
begging, the purse, the pipe, all of it came from the same place. You
decided I couldn\'t manage without you.\"

The boy\'s jaw set. \"I decided not to stand there while it went
wrong.\"

\"It wasn\'t going wrong. It was going slow. Learn the difference or
you\'ll die of the confusion.\" He said it flat, and let it sit, and
then, because the boy\'s stall had genuinely been a lovely piece of work
and lying about that served nobody: \"The purse was good. The purse
nearly held him. If you\'d sold it from the far side of the trough
instead of his own doorstep, it would have.\"

Odd Hands looked at him, suspicious of the kindness, cataloguing it for
traps.

\"What do we tell Sayd?\" he asked finally.

Tav stood. There was the question, and he watched himself walk past
every sensible answer to it.

\"You\'ll tell him whatever you tell him. If it were me, I\'d say you
held your post and the job ran clean.\" He settled his hat. \"Because
next week that\'ll be true, and I\'d rather train the thief you\'re
going to be than bury the one you were tonight.\"

He could have left the boy on the cobbles for the watch. The thought had
never seriously applied for the job. He knew what happened to raw,
unstructured talent in this city, had the mark on his chest to prove he
knew, and he\'d rather spend a year tolerating a reckless thief than
spend one morning wondering what they\'d ground him into.

It had worked out. Things generally did.

---

The trinkets came back two days later in a canvas bag, delivered by Odd
Hands to the room above the Buckled Mast. Tav put them away one at a
time, checking each by touch, the way you check a locked door even when
you watched yourself lock it. Copper mask. Carved crow. Brass disc. All
fourteen.

Getting there, Father.

He didn\'t take the ring out from under his shirt. It hadn\'t left his
chest in two years and it wasn\'t starting now. He turned it once
through the fabric and let it go.

### 2

Harlan\'s shop occupied the ground floor of a leaning building on
Carver\'s Lane, and the word shop was doing more work than the shop was.

Tav pushed through the door and the bell above it made a sound like a
cat being stepped on. The stock was thin. Half-empty shelves, goods that
had been dusted too often, a ledger on the counter trying very hard to
look like business was being done. The whole place smelled of tallow and
resignation.

Harlan was behind the counter, sorting inventory that didn\'t need
sorting. The years had done their work. The forearms were still large
and the shoulders still broad, but the rest of him had settled, and the
beard had gone more grey than brown. He looked up when the bell choked,
and his face went through three things in quick succession, the last of
which was something Tav couldn\'t read.

\"Tav.\"

\"Harlan.\"

They looked at each other across a counter of unsold goods.

\"You look like him,\" Harlan said, and then seemed to regret it, the
way people do when they\'ve said the true thing instead of the easy one.

\"Better looking, though.\"

Harlan\'s laugh came out before he could stop it, warm and sudden, and
it took ten years off him for about three counts. Then it was gone and
the shop was quiet again.

\"I need an inside man,\" Tav said, because he\'d never been good at
preamble and Harlan knew that about him. \"The Royal Procession is
stopping at Pendleton. I\'m going to take the vault. I need someone who
can stand in a room full of important people and look like he belongs
there.\"

\"No.\"

It came out flat and fast, before the pitch had even finished landing,
and it sat on the counter between them like a coin neither man wanted to
touch.

\"You haven\'t heard the plan.\"

\"I don\'t need to hear the plan.\" Harlan set down whatever he\'d been
pretending to sort. His hands rested flat on the wood, large and scarred
across the knuckles from work that predated the shop. \"Ten years, Tav.
I haven\'t run a job in ten years. I have a wife who thinks the worst
thing I\'ve ever done is undercharge for wicks. I have this.\" He
glanced around the shop, the thin shelves, the choking bell, and
something crossed his face that wasn\'t pride and wasn\'t far from it
either. \"It\'s not much. It\'s mine, and it\'s clean.\"

\"It\'s killing you. You\'ve got half a stockroom and a ledger you dust
more than you write in.\"

\"Men die of clean lives all the time.\" Harlan said it without heat.
\"Nobody hangs for one.\"

Tav looked at him. The face was stone, unreadable, professional, and the
eyes weren\'t, and that had always been the trouble with Harlan. He
could lie to a room and never to a person.

\"Alright.\" Tav took a candle from the near shelf, turned it once, set
a coin on the counter that was too large for it and didn\'t wait for
change. \"You know your own life.\"

He got as far as the door. Then, with his hand on it, because leaving a
thing wholly unsaid had never been among his talents:

\"It\'s his job I\'m finishing, Harlan. The painting\'s in that vault.
I\'m bringing it home.\" He didn\'t turn around for it. \"The Buckled
Mast, west side of the Warrens. Room over the kitchen. I\'m in Merranth
three more weeks.\"

The bell announced his exit like a small strangled protest, and he was
back on Carver\'s Lane with a candle he didn\'t need and the evening
coming down.

Three days, he gave it. Maybe four. People came around. They generally
did.

---

It took three.

The rain had set in that evening, the patient kind that meant to stay
the week, and Harlan came into the back room of the Buckled Mast with
his cap dripping and his beard newly trimmed, which told Tav everything
before either of them spoke. A man doesn\'t tidy himself to deliver a
second no.

\"You said the room over the kitchen.\"

\"I did. Sit. There\'s ale, or there\'s whatever this is.\" Tav nudged
the other cup. \"I haven\'t ruled out varnish.\"

Harlan sat. He didn\'t touch the cup. He put his cap on his knee and his
hands on the table, and he looked at Tav the way he\'d looked at him
across the counter, except the stone was gone from it and what remained
was older and harder to watch.

\"The whole plan,\" he said. \"Before I touch anything, I hear the whole
plan, and if any piece of it smells like your father\'s last one, I
walk, and you let me.\"

\"Agreed.\"

\"Two things more.\" Harlan held up a finger, workman\'s habit, counting
on his hands like the numbers might otherwise escape. \"One. Nobody
learns what I was. Not your crew, not your fence, not anyone. To them
I\'m a shopkeeper you\'ve hired for a face, and that\'s all I ever
was.\" The finger stayed up a moment. \"Ten years I\'ve been building
the man my wife married. He doesn\'t survive people knowing.\"

\"Done. And two?\"

\"When it\'s over, it\'s over. You take your painting and your money and
you sail, and you don\'t walk into my shop again.\" He said it steadily,
and it cost him, Tav could see it costing him, and he said it anyway.
\"It\'s nothing against you. A door only holds shut if nobody keeps
opening it.\"

The rain worked at the shutters. Tav turned his cup a half-turn on the
table.

\"My father used to say you could walk into a duke\'s study and the duke
would offer you his own chair.\"

\"Dukes are easy.\" Harlan picked up the ale after all and drank off a
third of it. \"It\'s wives that see through you.\"

\"Then it\'s done. Whole plan, no names, and after the job I\'m a
rumour.\" Tav put out his hand. \"It\'s good to have you, Harlan.\"

Harlan looked at the hand for a moment. Then he took it, warm and large
and steady, and the thing that crossed his face was gone before Tav
could put a word to it, and Tav didn\'t try very hard.

He walked Harlan out through the rain-noise of the taproom, and
afterward he stood at the window of the room over the kitchen and
watched the big man\'s shape move away down the lane, collar up,
unhurried, a tradesman heading home to his supper.

Two conditions, and both of them fences around the same small life. A
man drawing a line around what he loved. Tav could respect a line. He
intended to be well over the horizon before anyone had to test it.

He\'d given it three days. It had taken three. He turned the ring once
through his shirt and went to find some supper of his own.

### 3

Maddy arrived on a merchant sloop out of Dolliver, carrying a single
pack with more empty space than full, wearing the same grey overcoat
she\'d worn the last time he saw her, and looking at the harbour as if
it owed her money.

Tav watched her walk up the dock. She moved the way she always moved:
deliberate, unhurried, every stride an announcement.

\"You\'re staring,\" Odd Hands said from behind him.

\"I\'m observing. There\'s a professional difference. Make yourself
useful. Go tell your master we're ready."

Odd Hands looked at Maddy one more time, then disappeared into the dock
crowd with the ease of someone who\'d had practice at not being noticed.

---

She met him at the Buckled Mast and set her pack on the floor and looked
at the room the way a captain looks at a ship someone else had been
sailing.

\"It\'s smaller than you described.\"

\"I described it accurately. You imagined it larger.\"

\"I imagined it clean.\" She sat on the only chair and crossed one ankle
over her knee. \"Tell me where the plan is at.\"

He told her. The vault, the Procession, the timetable, Sayd\'s
involvement. She listened without interrupting, which was her way of
telling him she had objections and was saving them.

\"And the inside man?\"

\"Harlan. He was my father\'s---\"

\"I know who Harlan is.\" She said it flat. \"When do I meet him?\"

---

She met Harlan that evening in the back room of the Buckled Mast, and
Tav watched the introduction the way you watch two dogs circle each
other in a yard.

\"Harlan, this is Maddy. She\'s the best thief I\'ve ever worked with,
including myself, which I\'ll deny if either of you repeat it.\"

Harlan extended a hand. \"I knew his father.\"

\"He mentioned.\" Maddy took the hand, briefly. \"How long since you\'ve
worked?\"

\"Ten years, give or take.\"

\"Give or take what?\"

Harlan blinked. Tav almost felt sorry for him. Maddy asked questions the
way other people set traps, and she was unapologetic in her demeanor.

\"Ten years exactly,\" Harlan said, and his voice held steady, which
earned him something in her assessment. Not trust. Something adjacent.

The evening settled into planning. Routes, timelines, contingencies.
Maddy was thorough in a way that made Tav\'s instinct-first approach
look like a character flaw, which it probably was.

\"We\'re waiting on intelligence,\" Tav said. \"Vault layout, guard
rotation, whatever Sayd can get on what\'s behind those walls. And
there\'s one more coming with us. A boy. His apprentice.\"

\"Who\'s Sayd?\" Maddy asked.

Harlan looked up from the map. He was curious too.

\"Old friend.\" Tav moved on in a way that made it clear the subject was
closed. \"The boy\'s part of the arrangement. He comes through the doors
with us.\"

\"Someone else\'s eyes on our job,\" Maddy said.

\"Part of the deal.\"

\"Your deal.\" She said it without inflection, which was worse than any
tone she could have used.

She asked about the chartered ship, and he told her: a vessel booked
under a false name, departing Merranth harbour two days after the
Procession. Their exit.

\"And where does it go?\" she asked, and there was something in the
question that wasn\'t about geography.

\"Wherever we point the bow.\"

\"I have a destination in mind.\" She said it once, without explanation,
and moved on.

She turned from the window then, and her blue eyes found the ring worn
around his neck, the plain silver one, scratched and worn and very
obviously cared for.

She didn\'t say anything about it. She picked up the map of Pendleton
instead and started marking approaches, and if the corner of her mouth
moved a fraction, Tav was kind enough not to mention it.

Later, when Harlan had gone back to Carver\'s Lane, Maddy stood at the
window with her arms folded and her overcoat still on, because Maddy
always kept her coat on, and whether that was habit or policy was a
question Tav had never asked.

\"I don\'t trust him,\" she said.

\"You don\'t trust anyone.\"

\"I don\'t trust him specifically. There\'s a difference.\"

Tav put his hat over his face and lay back on the bed. \"He was my
father\'s partner. The man my father died to protect. He\'s solid.\"

\"People change over ten years.\"

\"Some people change over ten years.\"

She didn\'t push it. That was Maddy. She planted her flag and left it
standing and didn\'t repeat herself. You either heard her or you
didn\'t.

He heard her. He just didn\'t listen.

*Sloppy*, said the voice, very quietly, and he told it that Maddy was
cautious about everyone and this was no different.

### 4

A knock against the door drew Sayd\'s attention away from the looming
future. Odd Hands had learned to knock. That was progress.

\"Come in.\"

The boy entered and stood three paces from the desk. Ready to move, even
when there was nowhere to go. Sayd had noticed this the first time and
it had told him everything he needed to know about where the boy had
grown up.

\"The surveyor\'s plans,\" Sayd said.

\"Delivered. The seals are clean.\"

\"And?\"

\"He\'s fine.\" Odd Hands said it the way you\'d describe a meal that
didn\'t impress you. \"Slow on the locks. Careful. I held the street and
he got the plans. It ran clean.\"

\"Clean.\"

\"Sixty counts inside. Maybe less.\"

Sayd drew on the tabac and looked at the boy for exactly as long as it
took the boy to find something in the middle distance worth studying.
There was a bill going up along the harbour that morning with two
figures on it, a tall man in a distinctive coat and a boy of small
stature, and the watch did not paper the gates over a job that ran
clean. He considered pressing it, and set the thought down. The boy had
come home lying for the man he\'d been sent to study, ten days after
meeting him. That was worth more than the truth of the street, whatever
it had been, and it told Sayd everything about Tav he\'d been meaning to
check.

\"He\'s better than you think. Watch him at Pendleton. Really watch. And
if you still can\'t see it after that, I was wrong about you.\"

Odd Hands looked like he wanted to argue. He didn\'t. That was progress
too.

\"The credentials.\" Sayd opened a drawer and set a leather case on the
desk. \"Inspector\'s papers for the Pendleton advance sweep. Genuine
stock, forged signature, good enough for anything short of a direct
inquiry with the issuing office. The inside man will need to study the
protocol: how an inspector presents, what he examines, the language in
his report. I\'ve included notes.\"

\"And the intelligence?\"

\"Vault layout, guard rotation, a partial inventory.\" He slid a second
envelope across. \"This goes to Tav. Not to the inside man, not the
woman. Tav.\"

Odd Hands took both and lingered.

\"Go,\" Sayd said. \"And eat something. You look thin.\"

Odd Hands left. Sayd stayed at the window.

Tav had assembled his crew. The father\'s old partner, a woman from the
ports. Not badly done for a man who planned on instinct. The inside man
was the weak point, though Tav wouldn\'t see it that way. Ten years out
of practice, and men who left the life for honest work carried guilt
like cargo. Guilt made people unreliable. It made them unpredictable.

But that was Tav\'s problem.

Sayd\'s was simpler. Five years ago, Tav had walked out of the Accord.
The machine that makes men into Shadows, that grinds bones into
compliance, that has been running longer than anyone alive remembers
why. Nobody walks out. And after Pendleton, wherever Tav went, wherever
he kept his secrets, Sayd would know.

He reached for another pinch of tabac and waited, which was the thing he
did best.

### 5

The candle on the kitchen table was one of his own, which meant it
guttered.

Harlan sat with the ledger open in front of him and didn\'t read it.
Across the table Annis had his tunic over her knees and a needle going,
small quick stitches, her head bent to the light. The supper things were
dried and stacked. The rain had passed on toward the harbour and left
the lane quiet, and the only sounds in the room were the needle and the
candle spitting and, somewhere under the floor, the building settling
into another night of leaning.

\"You\'ve gone through the left elbow again,\" she said, without looking
up.

\"I lean on it.\"

\"I know you lean on it. I\'m the one who patches it.\" She turned the
sleeve, considered her work, went on. \"Mrs. Kellet was in today while
you were at the wharf. She asked me what\'s come over you.\"

\"Come over me.\"

\"You\'ve been whistling. Three days now. In the shop, in the yard. She
heard it through the wall and thought we\'d taken in a lodger.\" Annis
looked up then, and there was a smile at the edges of her, careful, the
way she\'d smile at good weather she didn\'t quite trust. \"Whatever
this shipment is, it agrees with you.\"

The shipment. He\'d built it for her the way he\'d once built a face for
a room: fine goods coming up from Dolliver, a seller who owed a man who
owed him, the kind of stroke of luck the shop had been waiting ten years
to be dealt. He\'d kept the details thin because details were where lies
went to die, and she hadn\'t pressed, because she trusted him, and that
was the whole rotten arithmetic of it.

\"It could turn the year around,\" he said, which had the advantage of
being true of several things at once.

\"I put by the carter\'s fee already. Out of the tin.\" She bit the
thread. \"So don\'t go paying it twice out of pride and telling me
after.\"

The candle spat. Harlan looked at the ledger without seeing it.

She had budgeted for it. His lie had walked into her arithmetic and been
given a seat, three shillings put by in the tin behind the flour, and
she\'d done it happily because for three days her husband had been
whistling. That was the part he hadn\'t planned for. Not the lying, he
could lie, God help him, he\'d been better at lying than at anything
he\'d done honestly since. It was that the whistling was real.

Ten years he\'d spent building the man she married. Sanding him,
patching him, leaning on him until the elbows went. And three days back
in the other life, one meeting in a tavern back room and a walk home in
the rain, and his own body had voted. He\'d caught himself reading the
street again on the way to the wharf. Counting exits in the
fishmonger\'s. Clocking a stranger\'s hands before his face. The old
sight, coming back like feeling into a numb limb, and it didn\'t ache.
That was the trouble. He\'d waited ten years for it to ache and instead
it fit.

\"You\'re not listening,\" Annis said.

\"I\'m listening. The tin. Don\'t pay twice.\"

She studied him a moment longer than the words needed. Whatever she was
looking for, she didn\'t find it, or she found it and filed it, he\'d
never once been sure which with her. Dukes are easy. She folded the
tunic over the chair back, smoothed the new patch flat with her thumb.

\"Come up soon,\" she said, and kissed the top of his head on the way
past, the way she\'d done for the better part of ten years, as though
his hair weren\'t going and the ledger weren\'t thin and the whole
leaning house weren\'t held up mostly by her refusal to notice. \"The
bed\'s cold on one side.\"

He listened to her on the stairs. Fourth tread, seventh tread. Then the
small sounds above, and then nothing.

He sat until the candle had burned down another finger\'s width. Then he
opened the drawer under the counter-end of the table, moved the ribbon
tin and the candle moulds, and took out the letter.

He didn\'t need to read it. The paper had gone soft at the folds,
cloth-soft, from being opened and closed and opened, and the words came
up on their own the way a smell does. Aldean\'s hand, neat as a
clerk\'s, a hand that had learned its letters in the same gutter school
Harlan had and made better use of them since.

Deliver something useful. Something I can take to the chief. And
there\'s a desk with your name on it.

A desk. A warrant card in a leather fold. Special Investigations, where
a man\'s talent for reading rooms and wearing faces was called aptitude
and drew a salary, and nobody ever had to know it had been apprenticed
elsewhere. Legitimate. That was Aldean\'s word for it, said across a
table two years ago, and Harlan had said nothing and gone home, and the
word had followed him like a stray. Not rich. Not forgiven. Legitimate.
A man his wife could believe in and be right.

Something useful.

He knew what the words were offering to mean, this week. He hadn\'t let
them finish saying it.

The shipment could still be only a shipment. That was the door he was
keeping open, sitting there in the gutter-light with the letter in his
hands: one job, one good take, the shop stocked and the tin full, and
Tav over the horizon like he\'d promised, and Aldean\'s letter could go
soft in the drawer for another two years. That was a future. He could
nearly see it. It had the look of the goods on his shelves, dusted
often, never sold.

He put the letter back under the ribbon tin and shut the drawer with
more care than shutting a drawer needed. Blew out the candle. Went up in
the dark, fourth tread, seventh tread, and lay down on his half of the
cold bed, and listened to Annis breathe.

Across the room, over the chair back, the tunic sat with its new patch.
She\'d matched the thread as near as the sewing box allowed. A shade
off. You\'d only see it if you were looking.

### 6

Maddy was waiting in the room over the kitchen, which was wrong twice.
Once because he hadn\'t told her he\'d taken it, and once because the
door had been locked.

She was sitting in the only chair with her boots on his table and a
paper in her hand, and she watched him hang up his hat before she said
anything, which was its own kind of sentence.

\"Something you\'d like to tell me?\"

\"The lock\'s for show, apparently.\"

\"The lock\'s fine. You\'re predictable.\" She held the paper out at
arm\'s length, not rising, so that he had to cross the room and take it.
\"This is nailed up at the harbour gate. And on Chandler Row. And
outside the fish market, where I stood and watched a man read it out
loud to six other men.\"

The bill was cheap stock and fresh ink. NOTICE, it said, in the tall
letters they save for money and crime, and below that the watch\'s
compliments and a description. Unlawful entry on Salt Street. A boy of
small stature, quick, dark-haired. And a tall fellow, lean, in a dark
travelling coat with blue at the collar. A reward that wouldn\'t keep a
drunk in ale for a week, and a line at the bottom inviting the public to
do its duty.

Tav read it twice. It didn\'t improve.

\"Well,\" he said. \"The good news is they\'ve flattered my height.\"

\"Talk.\"

He talked. The errand for Sayd, the surveyor, the boy coming off the
roof, the drainpipe\'s opinions, the woman with the lamp. He told it
straight and without varnish because Maddy could hear varnish the way
other people heard shouting, and when he finished she sat very still
with her ankles crossed on his table, and the silence had a temperature.

\"You ran a job,\" she said, \"in the city we\'re staging from. Three
weeks before the biggest take of your life. With somebody else\'s
apprentice, on somebody else\'s errand, wearing that coat.\"

\"The seals were clean. Nothing\'s missing as far as any of them know.
That bill says entry, not theft, which means the surveyor counted his
papers and found all of them. They\'re looking for two people who took
nothing.\" He tossed the bill onto the table by her boots. \"It\'s a
coat in a city of coats, Maddy.\"

\"It\'s your coat.\" She came off the chair, took up the bill, and held
it beside his collar like a draper matching cloth. \"Blue at the collar.
I found you on a dock in Vessara across a hundred yards of crowd by this
lining. You think a watchman can\'t do it across a street?\"

\"You were looking for me.\"

\"So are they now. That\'s the point I\'m making.\" She let the bill
fall on the table. \"And here\'s the other one. Your man Sayd\'s notes
said the surveyor was regular. Home by dark, church-sober. He came home
drunk at midnight and nearly stood on you. Once is a bad night. But
you\'re about to walk into a vault in Pendleton on that same man\'s
intelligence, and I\'d like to know which of his facts is the next bad
night.\"

There it was. He\'d filed the surveyor\'s habits away in the drawer of
things Sayd had been wrong about, and told himself the drawer had one
item in it, and here was Maddy pulling the drawer clean out of the desk
and showing him it could hold plenty.

\"Sayd\'s intelligence bought us the layout and the rotation,\" he said.
\"Without it there\'s no job.\"

\"I know. That\'s what worries me.\" She went to the window and looked
down at the lane, arms folded. \"The coat goes away. Until we sail. Wear
something that doesn\'t have a signature.\"

He was quiet a moment too long, and she turned around at the quality of
it.

\"It\'s a coat, Tav.\"

It wasn\'t, quite, but the argument for that was made of nothing he
could put on a table. It was the coat he\'d bought in his first good
year at sea. The coat he\'d been wearing on the dock when she found him
across a hundred yards of crowd. It had been through twenty-six ports
with him, same as the trinkets, and it went in the same ledger: things
that fixed a moment in place and said I was here.

\"Until we sail,\" he said.

\"Until we sail.\" She looked at him a moment longer, and something in
her stood down by a degree. \"And I want to know who Sayd is. Not the
sermon. The facts. If my neck is resting on his paperwork, I\'ll satisfy
myself about the man.\"

\"That\'s between him and me.\"

\"Then I\'ll satisfy myself my own way.\" She said it without heat, the
way she said everything that was already decided, and picked up her pack
from beside the chair. At the door she paused, not turning. \"Lose the
coat tonight. Not tomorrow.\"

He listened to her on the stairs until the taproom noise took her. Then
he stood in the middle of the room and looked at the coat on its hook,
dark cloth, the blue lining showing at the collar the way it always did,
the way it had across a hundred yards of Vessaran crowd.

He folded the lining under and buttoned the collar flat, and it looked
like any coat in Merranth, more or less, and he told himself that
counted.

---

The watchman came into the shop a little before closing, and the bell
had its usual opinion.

Harlan looked up from the counter, and his face did what ten years of
shopkeeping had taught it, which was to be pleased about a customer. It
stayed pleased while he clocked the uniform, the bill in the man\'s
fist, the tired shape of a fellow at the end of a long round of
doorways.

\"Evening,\" the watchman said. \"Won\'t keep you. There\'s been an
entry over on Salt Street, and we\'re asking along the lanes.\" He laid
the bill on the counter and turned it to face Harlan, a courtesy he\'d
clearly performed forty times today. \"A boy, and a tall fellow in a
dark coat. Blue at the collar. Anything like that come through?\"

Harlan read the bill. He took his time about it, a shopkeeper being
thorough for the watch, and while his eyes went down the lines the rest
of him stood in a room three weeks ago at the Buckled Mast, watching
rain drip off a cap, across a table from a dark travelling coat with
blue showing at the collar. The lining had caught the lamplight when Tav
leaned in. He remembered thinking it was a young man\'s coat.

\"Blue at the collar,\" he said, considering it. \"No. I\'d remember
that. Nothing so fine comes down Carver\'s Lane, more\'s the pity for
the ledger.\" He slid the bill back. \"Boys of small stature I can offer
you by the dozen, but that\'s the Warrens for you.\"

The watchman\'s mouth acknowledged the joke without committing to it.
\"If anything like it turns up.\" He left a second copy of the bill on
the counter. \"Post it where folk can see, if you would. People read a
window better than they listen to a watchman.\"

The bell strangled itself over the man\'s exit.

Harlan stood with the bill in his hands. The lie had come out seamless,
unhurried, warm. It had cost him nothing at all. There had been a small
clean click in the middle of it, the old click, the tumbler of a room
turning over, and he\'d felt it with the same part of himself that had
been whistling for a week, and he stood there hating how little he hated
it.

He found the paste pot under the counter. He smoothed the bill flat
against the inside of the window glass, facing the lane, square and
civic and helpful, and pressed the corners down with his thumb.

It was a good likeness, as words went. Anyone who knew the coat would
know it straight off.

He went back to the counter and the inventory that didn\'t need sorting,
and if he kept glancing at the window while the light died, that was
only a shopkeeper minding his glass.

### 7

They planned in the back room of the Buckled Mast with the door locked
and the windows shuttered and Odd Hands posted in the hall, because
somebody had to stand in the cold and Tav had decided it built
character.

The vault plans covered the table. Sayd\'s intelligence was pinned to
the wall. The guard rotation stood in Maddy\'s tight handwriting down
one margin, which was the neatest thing in the room including the
furniture, and beside the door, propped against the wall where nobody
could pretend not to see it, was the bill from the harbour gate. Maddy
had brought it. She hadn\'t said why. She hadn\'t needed to.

\"The watch came down Carver\'s Lane yesterday,\" Harlan said. He said
it early, before anything else was on the table, the way a man declares
his cargo at the gate. \"House to house with that bill. I sent the
fellow off happy and posted it in my window like a good citizen.\"

\"How wide are they asking?\" Maddy said.

\"Salt Street outward. The lanes, the market rows. He had the look of a
man doing the whole quarter.\" Harlan laced his fingers on the table.
\"It\'s routine. An entry with nothing taken doesn\'t buy more than a
week of asking. But the harbour gate\'s a different matter. A bill at
the harbour gate means somebody thinks the pair of them might sail, and
bills at gates don\'t come down. They fade there. Men who stand watch at
gates get bored, and bored men read their walls.\"

Tav looked at him. Ten years behind a counter and the man had just laid
out the watch\'s habits like a fishwife pricing the morning\'s catch.
Maddy was looking at him too, and for once Tav couldn\'t read what her
look was doing.

\"Then we give them nothing to match,\" Tav said. \"The coat\'s retired.
The boy stays out of the harbour district. In three weeks the ink\'s
grey and we\'re a smell on the tide.\"

\"Two problems with the tide,\" Maddy said.

She came forward and put her finger on the harbour end of the Merranth
map, on the neat little drawing of the gate where her bill had been
nailed.

\"One. Our exit sails from a harbour where the watch has fresh reason to
look at faces. Two.\" She dragged her finger south, off the map\'s edge.
\"There\'s a cleaner road. We come out of Pendleton and we don\'t come
back here at all. Overland, south, four days to Dolliver. I know
Dolliver, I know which harbourmen count and which just take the fee. We
ship from there under names nobody in Merranth ever heard, and this
whole city can keep its bill.\"

\"Four days on the road with the take,\" Tav said.

\"Yes.\"

\"Several hundred pounds\' weight of it, on horses, through toll posts,
the week after the Pendleton repository gets opened like an egg. Every
road out of that town will have a man on it counting wagons by the
second morning. The roads are where they\'ll look, Maddy, because roads
are slow and searchable and the law loves a thing it can stand on. The
sea\'s a door that shuts.\" He tapped the harbour. \"The charter\'s
booked under a name that doesn\'t exist, out of a harbour that sees
forty sailings a week. We board with the morning crowd, and by the time
Pendleton\'s counted its silver we\'re a sail. Nobody searches the
horizon.\"

\"They don\'t have to search the horizon. They have to search a gangway,
once, on the right morning.\"

\"On a morning when they\'re looking for two men who robbed a surveyor
of nothing.\" He shook his head. \"It\'s a good road, yours. It\'s
slower and it\'s watched in different places, and it means four days of
luck instead of one hour of it. I\'d rather buy the hour.\"

The room held it a moment. Harlan turned the argument over with his eyes
on the map, and Tav watched him weigh it, genuinely weigh it, the old
operator\'s arithmetic running behind the shopkeeper\'s face.

\"The ship,\" Harlan said at last. \"He\'s right about roads after a job
that size. I\'ve seen the county turned out to walk fields for less.
Water\'s the only door that shuts behind you.\" He glanced at Maddy, and
it was almost apologetic. \"But he\'s wrong that it\'s one hour of luck.
It\'s one hour of luck at a place they\'ve already papered. I\'d want a
second berth held, later tide, different dock, under a third name. A
door behind the door.\"

\"Then that\'s yours,\" Maddy said. \"Arrange it.\"

\"I can do that.\"

She looked at the two of them, one to the other, and whatever she did
with the count of it stayed off her face. Then she pulled the Pendleton
plans across and the argument was over, the way arguments with Maddy
ended, filed rather than settled.

\"The lower access,\" she said. \"Sayd\'s layout shows a service passage
at foundation level. You\'re building the whole entry on it, so I\'ll
ask once. How does he know it\'s open? Because his information has been
wrong before, and I\'ve marked the margin where it was.\"

She had, too. There was a small tight annotation against the guard
rotation, and Tav didn\'t need to lean over to know what it said.

\"The passage predates the vault. It\'s on the surveyed foundation
plans, and the bank\'s own renovations never touched foundation level,
that\'s in the deposit records Sayd pulled. Stone doesn\'t move because
nobody\'s looked at it.\"

\"Grates rust shut. Passages get bricked when a clerk gets nervous.
Records say what the last man wrote, and the last man wrote it before we
were born.\" She held his eye. \"If we come down to foundation level the
night of, with the take on our backs, and that grate doesn\'t give?\"

\"Then I open it. That\'s the job, Maddy. Somewhere there\'s always a
door that\'s supposed to be a wall, and I\'m the one who argues with
it.\" He grinned, and put no weight on it, and underneath the grin he
marked the question in his own margin, in the drawer with the
surveyor\'s drinking habits, and shut the drawer the way he always shut
it.

\"The advance sweep confirms it,\" Harlan said quietly. \"That\'s what
the sweep is for. I keep the floor busy, and he stands on the access
point long enough to know it\'s real. We don\'t leave Pendleton the
first time without the answer.\"

Maddy considered that, and gave it a short nod, which from her was a
signature on the arrangement.

They worked the rest of it down to the floorboards. Routes and timings,
the second bell and the third, who carried what and in which order, and
Maddy was thorough in a way that made Tav\'s instinct-first approach
look like a character flaw, which it probably was. He didn\'t lay out
everything. Not the sequence through the lower level, not the
countermeasures, because some of it he hadn\'t figured out yet and
wouldn\'t until he was standing in the room itself, which was how Tav
worked and would probably be carved on his headstone as a cautionary
tale. Maddy knew this about him. She\'d stopped trying to fix it around
the second job they\'d ever run, out of acceptance or exhaustion, he\'d
never asked which.

\"Your father would have liked this one,\" Harlan said, without looking
up from the plans. His big hands were flat on the vault\'s outline, and
his voice had gone somewhere private to fetch the words.

Tav didn\'t answer. He didn\'t need to.

Something passed between them over the table. Tav thought of it as
understanding.

---

They finished after midnight. Harlan took the credentials and the
protocol notes and left for Carver\'s Lane, walking slow, and Odd Hands
materialized from the hall and followed. Tav let him. The boy was going
to follow people regardless; better he practice on someone who wouldn\'t
notice.

Maddy stood at the window. Cinnamon and cold air. She had her overcoat
off for once, the white shirt loose, and she was looking out at nothing
like it owed her an explanation.

\"You\'re thinking,\" he said.

\"I\'m always thinking.\"

\"Louder than usual tonight.\"

She didn\'t turn around. \"Do you remember Vessara? The night on the
dock when we talked about what we\'d do if we ever stopped running.\"

He remembered. Two years ago, a bottle of something cheap between them,
and Maddy talking about ruins. Lost places. Cities that existed only in
fragments of text nobody had bothered to connect. He\'d laughed and
called them treasure hunters. She hadn\'t laughed. She\'d said *someone
should go and look*, and the way she\'d said it had made him realize she
wasn\'t joking.

\"You\'re still thinking about that.\"

\"I found it.\" She turned then. \"Three texts. A Nemogali shipping log,
a monastery record from the Reach, and a surveyor\'s note buried in an
archive in Dolliver. They don\'t know about each other. But they
describe the same place. A settlement, old, abandoned, untouched.
Nobody\'s connected them because nobody\'s been looking.\"

\"Except you.\"

\"Except me.\" Her reflection in the glass was clearer than her voice.
\"I'm going whether you came or not. I chose to ask you."

\"Ask,\" Tav said. \"Is that what that was?\"

\"I presented an opportunity.\"

\"An opportunity you need me for.\"

\"I don\'t really need you.\"

\"Like that time you told me to be on a dock at a specific time with my
things packed?\"

\"And you showed up.\"

\"I\'m very agreeable.\"

\"You\'re very easy to manage, you mean.\"

He laughed. A real one, the kind that escaped before he could decide
whether to let it. She did that to him. Found the seam between the
performance and the person and picked it open like a lock he didn\'t
know he\'d left unguarded.

\"Tell me about the ruins.\"

\"You don\'t care about ruins.\"

He turned the ring through the fabric of his shirt. \"My father used to
tell me something his father told him. \'*See a thing before someone
tells you what it is. That\'s the only honest look you\'ll ever
get.*\'\"

He let that sit.

\"So yeah. I care.\"

She looked at him for a beat longer than professional. Then she turned
back to the window and talked. Not the way she usually talked, clipped
and rationed, always aware of what she was giving away.

The monastery record described a settlement in the mountains above the
Reach. Old. The Nemogali shipping log listed cargo bound for a port that
didn\'t appear on any modern chart. And the surveyor\'s note, buried in
an archive in Dolliver, mentioned foundations visible from a ridge that
the surveyor hadn\'t been paid enough to investigate.

\"Three people saw the edges of the same thing from three different
directions,\" she said. \"None of them knew what they were looking at.\"

\"And you connected them.\"

\"Nobody else bothered to look.\" She traced a ridgeline on the window
glass with one finger. \"Do you know how rare that is? A place that
hasn\'t been picked over, catalogued, sold to a patron or locked in a
vault? Somewhere that\'s just *there*, waiting for the first person
stubborn enough to find it?\"

\"A whole city where nobody\'s touched anything,\" Tav said. \"Where
everything is exactly where someone left it, and the dust hasn\'t been
disturbed, and you\'re the first eyes on it in however many centuries.\"
He was quiet for a moment. She turned and looked at him, and there was
nothing in her face for once but the place she was describing, no past
in it, no debts, nobody\'s mark on anything. Her guard was entirely
down, which happened so rarely that he\'d learned to hold still when it
did, the way you hold still for a bird that\'s landed on the sill and
hasn\'t decided whether to stay.

\"You know what I can\'t figure out about you?\" she said.

\"My boundless appeal?\"

\"You\'re smart. You read rooms, you read people, you're ahead of the
game when it matters. But you walk through the world like none of it can
touch you. Like the worst thing that could happen is something you\'ll
handle when it arrives.\"

\"That\'s optimism.\"

\"That\'s recklessness.\"

\"Fine line. I like my side of it.\"

\"There is no line, Tav." She folded her arms, but loosely, not the
locked-down fold she used on the world. \"And you never ask for
anything. You just show up and assume people will come with you. And the
worst part is, they do.\"

\"You\'re here,\" he said. Quietly. Without the grin.

\"I\'m here. With charts and years of research and a plan for what
happens after. And you showed up with a hat and a smile and a perfect
heist that doesn\'t have an ending yet.\"

\"It has an ending. The ending is the ruins. The ending is you and me
standing in a place nobody\'s seen in centuries, arguing about which
direction to go first.\"

\"I wouldn\'t argue. I\'d already know.\"

\"And I\'d go the wrong way on purpose, and find something better.\"

\"Or you'd spring a trap and somehow end up in a jail with no guards.\"

She sat beside him on the bed. Close enough that he could feel the
warmth through her sleeve. They didn\'t touch. They didn\'t talk. The
lamp did its quiet work and the room was small and for a few minutes
neither of them needed to be anywhere other than exactly where they
were.

---

Harlan saw them from the hallway.

He\'d come back for the protocol notes he\'d left on the table, and the
door was open a crack, and he stood there for three counts. Long enough.
Two people sitting on a bed in the lamplight, not touching, everything
unsaid between them.

He thought of his wife. Her face when he came home stinking of animal
lard and failure. The shop, the bell, the ten years of trying to be the
man she married.

He picked up the notes without a sound and left.

On the walk home the streets were empty and the cold was the kind that
got into your coat and stayed. He thought about the vault and the
painting and the dead man\'s son sitting in that room with a girl who
loved him and a plan that would either save them or ruin them. He
thought about Aldean Blundo\'s offer, sitting in a drawer at the shop
like an unpaid invoice. *Deliver something useful. Something I can take
to the chief. And there\'s a desk with your name on it.*

He\'d never answered. He\'d never thrown it away. The paper had gone
soft at the folds.

He walked home in the cold and didn\'t sleep, and when his wife asked
him what was wrong he said *nothing*, and she believed him, which was
the easiest and the worst part.

### 8

Pendleton was older than Merranth and quieter about it. The kind of town
that hadn\'t changed with the times. It welcomed the people who were
born there, watched them grow old, and outlasted every one of them.

The bank sat at the top of the high street. Stone walls, iron-banded
doors, a reputation for discretion that had survived two regime changes
and a civil war. The guards at the main gate looked like men who had
been told to take their work seriously and had taken the instruction
personally.

Harlan presented the credentials at the gate. He did it the way he did
everything: easily, warmly, with a hand extended and a name remembered
and a question about the stonework over the lintel that was genuinely
interested and not at all suspicious. The guards relaxed in his
presence. One of them offered tea.

\"For a grey beard who\'s out of practice, he\'s good,\" Odd Hands
murmured from behind Tav\'s shoulder.

\"Used to be one of the best.\"

There was a ledger at the gate, because of course there was, and they
signed it in the false names off Sayd\'s paper: the inspector and his
two assistants, the date, the hour, the purpose of visit written in
Harlan\'s steady tradesman\'s hand. Advance security survey, Royal
Procession. Tav signed a name that wasn\'t his under a name that wasn\'t
Harlan\'s and thought, not for the first time, that half of thieving was
writing fiction in other people\'s books.

They got as far as the main hall before the fiction met its first
reader.

The under-manager was a small precise man named Pellow who wore his keys
on a chain and his suspicion the same way, openly, as a matter of
professional pride. He read the credentials at the length of a document
he intended to remember. Then he read them again.

\"You\'ll forgive me,\" Pellow said, in the tone of a man who had never
once needed forgiving, \"but with the Procession so near, the bank\'s
policy is verification. I\'ll send a note to the issuing office this
afternoon confirming the commission. A formality. I\'m sure you
understand.\"

Beside Tav, Odd Hands had gone very still, in the way of a boy working
out the distance to the door. Genuine enough to survive a careful look,
Sayd had said. Anything short of a direct inquiry with the issuing
office.

Harlan smiled.

\"I\'d be disappointed in you if you didn\'t,\" he said. \"Write it
today, and mark it urgent, because the marshal\'s office is slow as
church business and the Procession won\'t wait on their clerks. Only do
me one kindness.\" He leaned in, confiding, one professional to another.
\"Put your own name to it clearly. When the marshal asks me why the
advance survey of his most important stop ran a day behind, I\'ll want
to tell him it was because Pendleton had a man thorough enough to check.
That sort of thing gets remembered in the right rooms, Mr. Pellow. I\'ll
see it\'s remembered correctly.\"

Tav watched the arithmetic move through Pellow like weather. Diligence,
praised. Delay, owned. His name, travelling upward toward important
rooms, attached to either a compliment or a bottleneck, and the choice
of which sitting entirely in his own inkwell.

\"Perhaps,\" Pellow said, \"verification can follow the survey rather
than precede it. Given the timetable.\"

\"Sensible man. That\'s going in the report as well.\"

They were escorted through the main hall and into a staircase that went
down into the deep, and Tav revised his estimate of Harlan for the third
time in a week. He hadn\'t talked his way past the check. He\'d made the
check work for him, turned the bank\'s own caution into a reason to
hurry, and the whole thing had cost him one smile and thirty seconds.
Ten years selling candles. It should have been a crime in itself.

The air changed as they went down. Cooler, drier. It kept the particular
stillness of a room that existed to keep things the same. The vault door
was thick iron and oak and took two men to move, and the sound it made
opening was something Tav would remember for a while.

The interior chamber was large, larger than the Buckled Mast\'s entire
ground floor. Oil lamps set into the walls. Alcoves lined with
strongboxes, cases, wrapped parcels, locked chests. The collected
portable wealth of the merchant class, and whatever nobility thought
their possessions safer here than at home.

They worked it as rehearsed. Harlan led, methodical, unhurried, making
notes on a clipboard with the attention of a man doing exactly what he
was there to do, and keeping the escorting officer inside a conversation
the way a shepherd keeps a dog inside a whistle. Tav walked the walls
with his own clipboard, counted alcoves, measured distances by stride,
and filed it all in the part of his mind that would reassemble it later
in the back room of the Buckled Mast. Odd Hands trailed him with a
satchel and an apprentice\'s blank face, and his eyes went everywhere
and his hands went nowhere, which for Odd Hands was the performance of a
lifetime.

At the far end, a stair ran down to the overflow level, and Tav stopped
at the head of it and frowned at the wall like a man being paid to frown
at walls.

\"Damp,\" he said, to nobody in particular, and then, to the officer:
\"There\'s damp coming up this stair. Has the foundation level been
surveyed since the river walls went in?\"

The officer didn\'t know. The officer was made uneasy by not knowing,
with the Procession coming. It took very little to turn unease into an
escort down the stair, Harlan asking the man something involved about
load-bearing walls as they went, and then Tav was standing in the
overflow chamber, a low stone room of crates and shelf-stacks and one
wall that mattered.

The grate was behind the third rank of crates, where the plans said it
would be. Old iron, a hand-span of rust at every joint, set into the
wall at knee height and forgotten by everyone who had ever been paid to
remember it. Tav crouched at the wall beside it, ran his hand along the
stone as though tracing water, and read the thing the way he read locks.
Hinged on the left. Fixed with four bolts, and two of the four were rust
more than bolt. It would open. It would complain, and then it would
open.

\"No damp,\" he announced, standing, dusting his knee. \"Condensation
off the stair iron. I\'ll note it as sound.\"

Forty counts, start to finish. Harlan\'s voice floated down the stair
the whole time, warm and endless, a question about medieval footings
that the officer would still be answering when the Procession arrived.

They came back up, and Tav returned to his striding and counting, and
that was when he found the third alcove on the west wall.

The Long View hung between a merchant\'s silver collection and a stack
of deed boxes. Simple frame, dark wood, no gilding. A landscape: hills,
a valley, a treeline catching late afternoon light. It was not large. It
was not, to any eye that lacked context, remarkable.

The vault catalogue had it listed as middling value. A minor noble\'s
deposit. One entry among hundreds.

Tav stood in front of it and the vault went away.

The treeline. It broke along the ridge in a way that nagged at him, not
quite random, not quite regular, as if the painter had been recording
something other than trees. He leaned closer. Ten years ago it had hung
on a different wall, and a man had taken it down in the dark, and that
man had held it the way Tav wanted to hold it now. Had died three days
later without giving up his partner\'s name, and the painting had ended
up here, filed like an invoice, forgotten by everyone except the son
who\'d come to take it home.

This is what you died for. This is what I came back for.

His hands wanted the frame. Not yet.

He stepped back. Made a note on his clipboard, a real one, in the false
inspector\'s hand, and moved to the next alcove, and the shadows cast by
lamplight hid whatever his face was doing.

---

Harlan had stopped moving.

He was standing in front of the painting, two alcoves back, and his
hands were at his sides, and the clipboard hung loose, and his face had
gone somewhere Tav couldn\'t see because Tav was already past him.

He\'d last seen this painting ten years ago. Different hands carrying
it. Hands that belonged to a man who\'d told him to run, and he\'d run,
and the man had died three days later in a room Harlan would never see,
refusing to speak Harlan\'s name under what the reports described only
as considerable questioning.

The painting had ended up here. Of course it had. Filed like an invoice.
Hanging between silverware and deeds. The noble who\'d recovered it
hadn\'t even displayed it. He\'d stuffed it in a vault and forgotten it
the way you forget a settled score.

And now the dead man\'s son was going to steal it back. With the dead
man\'s partner holding the door open.

Harlan looked at the painting. The treeline, the valley, the light. He
thought about the boy sitting on that bed in the lamplight with the girl
beside him. He thought about Aldean\'s letter in the drawer, soft at the
folds. He thought about what kind of man walks into a vault with a dead
friend\'s son and walks out a traitor.

He thought about what kind of man walks out anything else.

He picked up his clipboard. His face settled. The warmth drained out of
it slowly, like water through sand, and what was left behind was
professional, competent, and made of something that wouldn\'t change its
mind.

He went back to measuring the room.

---

When they left Pendleton late that afternoon, walking down the high
street with the bank\'s compliments and a signed copy of their own
survey report, Tav clapped him on the shoulder.

\"Good work in there. You\'ve still got it.\"

\"Never lost it, it seems,\" Harlan said. \"That\'s the problem.\"

Tav laughed. Harlan didn\'t.

Maddy was waiting at the inn where they\'d stabled the horses, arms
folded, watching Harlan come down the street with an expression Tav
didn\'t read because he wasn\'t looking at her. He was looking at the
road ahead, at the plan taking shape, at the vault sitting fat and
trusting at the top of the hill.

It was going to be perfect.

Maddy watched Harlan pass her without a word. Something about the way he
held his shoulders. Something about the way he didn\'t look back at the
bank on the hill. She\'d seen men change their minds before, in ports
and on ships and in back rooms where deals went wrong. It looked like
this. It looked like a man who had stopped carrying a weight and started
carrying a purpose, and the difference was only visible if you knew to
look for it.

She knew to look for it.

She said nothing, because she\'d already said it once, and Maddy didn\'t
repeat herself.

---

That night, in the back room of the Buckled Mast, Harlan fed the papers
to the stove one at a time. Sweep protocol, Sayd\'s notes on
inspectorial manner, the forged commission itself, each of them held by
a corner until the flame had a proper grip. He did it the way he did
everything now, thoroughly, and the room went briefly orange with other
people\'s names.

\"Passes too,\" he said. \"All three.\"

Odd Hands surrendered his without ceremony. Tav took his own from the
inner pocket and had it halfway to the stove when his hand made a
decision his head was still drafting.

It was good paper. Heavy stock, the Procession\'s seal pressed deep
enough to read by thumb, a false name written in a clerk\'s hand over a
commission that had walked him into the room where his father\'s
painting hung. Every job in the pack upstairs had its token. A mask, a
crow, a disc. Things that fixed a moment in place and said I was here,
and this happened, and it mattered.

The perfect heist should have the perfect token. Not something taken
from the vault. Something the job itself had made.

\"Tav,\" Harlan said.

\"Burned,\" Tav said, and passed his empty hand over the flames, and the
pass stayed flat against his palm and slid back into the inner pocket
with the ease of long practice, and neither of the other two caught it,
because he was, when it came down to it, very good.

He\'d burn it on the ship. Once it had been where the trinkets had been,
once the story was finished and the token had its ending. Paper burned
anywhere. There was no hurry about paper.

Things generally kept, if you kept them carefully.

### 9

Two notes sat on Sayd\'s desk, and between them they described his
entire situation, which was why he had not yet decided which to answer
first.

The first had come up from the street that morning by a hand he had not
enjoyed watching. The man had stood in the doorway without breathing
loudly enough to hear, had looked at nothing in the room while the note
changed hands, and the room had been quietly glad when he left. The note
itself was unsigned, in a clerk\'s grey script, and itemized services
rendered: the loan of two quiet servants, one night\'s work, the month
prior. A figure followed. The figure was modest. Their figures always
were, at first, and the note\'s closing courtesy presumed, without quite
saying so, the pleasure of his future custom.

The second note was cream stock and smelled faintly of Lord Casvern\'s
secretary. His lordship required his Factor\'s attendance at supper, two
nights hence, where the Vellisham brothers would be dining. Required was
not the word used. The word used was hoped, and it was doing the work of
required, and both of them knew it. Casvern liked to seat Sayd where new
money could look at him. A Factor of visible quality was a kind of
plate, brought out and polished and counted afterward, and lately, Sayd
had noticed, counted a little more carefully than before.

He stood at the window with the tabac burning slow between two fingers
and looked down at the harbour doing its morning impression of a
painting. One master who owned his evenings, and one who believed itself
owed his nights, and neither with the least suspicion that it was being
studied by the thing it thought it held. Patience was a currency. He had
been rich in it for years, and the account was nearly due.

Beyond the door, someone took a long breath, and then knocked. Progress
had held.

\"Come in.\"

Odd Hands crossed to his usual three paces from the desk and stood ready
to move, though the report clearly wanted to move first. Sayd let him
hold it a moment on principle.

\"Pendleton,\" he said, when the boy had suffered enough.

\"Clean. Signed in, swept, signed out, tea from the guards on the way
through.\" A pause, in which the boy visibly reorganized his account
into its proper order, which was itself new. \"There was a moment at the
start. An under-manager wanted to write the issuing office about the
commission. Verification.\"

\"And?\"

\"And the big man thanked him for it.\" Odd Hands said it with the frown
of someone still auditing the memory. \"Told him to send the letter,
urgent, with his own name writ large, so the marshal would know exactly
which clerk to credit for the delay to the survey. By the end the fellow
decided the letter could follow the visit instead of leading it, and he
thought it was his own idea, and he was grateful. It took half a
minute.\" The frown deepened. \"You can\'t even call it a lie. Every
word of it was true.\"

\"The best doors are opened with the truth. It\'s cheaper than picking
and it leaves no scratches.\" Sayd drew on the tabac. \"And Tav?\"

\"Found what he needed. He read the whole floor by walking it. And there
was a wall he wanted, down a level, so he made up an excuse about damp
stone and had a guard escort him to it, and he was at the wall forty
counts and came back with everything.\" The boy\'s weight shifted, and
what came out next came out grudgingly, the exact tone of a debt being
paid. \"You were right. He\'s better than I thought. He\'s better than
he looks like he is, which I think is the trick of it. He looks like
he\'s strolling.\"

\"He is strolling. He\'s simply doing it through rooms the rest of us
have to work in.\" Sayd tipped ash. \"What else?\"

\"Nothing else. He counted alcoves, made his notes.\" A shrug arrived
and thought better of itself halfway. \"He stopped at a picture for a
while. Some landscape, hills and trees. Stood in front of it like a man
in church. I thought he\'d been spotted at first, the way he\'d gone
still.\"

\"A picture.\"

\"Middling thing, by the tag. It\'s a vault full of silver and he stood
looking at trees.\"

Sayd smiled, briefly and without much in it. The trinkets on the shelf
had told him everything five years of absence hadn\'t: fourteen
worthless objects, carried across half the world for no other reason
than sentiment. It was the defining difference between them now.
Sentiment. It would cost Tav something someday, and when it did, it
would be no concern of Sayd's.

\"The woman,\" Sayd said. \"Halst tells me somebody has been asking
after me in Lowertown. At the Coiner\'s, at two separate taverns, and of
a moneylender who had the sense to send word. Careful questions.
Professional ones. Whoever it was wanted my business, my history, and my
reputation for keeping terms, and wanted them in that order.\"

Odd Hands blinked. \"The crew woman? The one from Dolliver?\"

\"Doing her diligence before she rests her neck on my paperwork.\" And
she had cause, which was the irritating part. The surveyor\'s habits had
been wrong in his notes, the sort of error that crept in around
unimportant men, and she had evidently decided that a man wrong about
small things wanted checking on the large ones. Sound reasoning. He\'d
have done the same. In five weeks, Tav had never once asked a question
about him. She had needed five days.

\"Let her ask. See that she finds the truth, the boring parts of it, in
the usual places. A woman that thorough gets suspicious of a surface she
can\'t scratch, so give her a scratch or two. Nothing below the paint.\"

\"And Tav? Does he hear about it?\"

\"No. Tav operates on sentiment; it is his nature to trust those he
suffered with. Pointing out her suspicions will only force him to choose
between her caution and our history. I prefer he remains blind to both,
as I have a great interest in his plan.\" He set the tabac down. \"Which
brings me to your work. Pendleton night.

\"I\'m through the doors with them. That\'s arranged.\"

\"You are. And when it\'s over, and the take is split, and everyone is
walking away in their different directions, you will be on Tav. Not the
crew. Not the woman. Him.\" Sayd came around the desk. \"Wherever he
goes first, before the ship, before anything. You do not stop him, you
do not speak to him, you are a shadow on a wall. A man who has just won
tells you everything worth knowing by where he walks first, because he
stops choosing and starts wanting. Wherever Tav walks that night, that
is where five years of my curiosity lives. Do you understand what I am
telling you to bring me?\"

\"An address,\" Odd Hands said.

\"An address.\" Sayd nodded at the door. \"Go. Eat something first."

The boy went. Sayd stood a while at the window, and the harbour
glittered below, ships at anchor sitting still as painted things, and he
took up the Accord\'s grey little invoice and read the figure again,
modest, courteous, patient, and set it under the paperweight with the
care one gives a thing that will matter later.

After Pendleton, the address. After the address, the method. And after
the method, there would be a morning, he had promised himself this for
five years, when the grey notes stopped arriving as invoices and started
arriving as reports.

He answered Casvern\'s supper first. Hoped, he wrote, was too weak a
word for his anticipation. Let the plate be counted. It would not be
plate much longer.

### 10

The chop-house stood two streets back from the Special Investigations
offices, and it fed clerks, which meant it was cheap, quick, and full of
men trained not to hear the next table. Aldean had picked it. Aldean had
always been the one who picked.

He was there ahead of Harlan, at the corner table, and the sight of him
crossed ten years in the time it took to hang a coat. Neat as a ledger
line. Ink on the middle finger where the pen sat, grey coming in at the
temples the orderly way, one row at a time. He\'d been the smallest of
the lane boys and the only one who could read, and he\'d climbed out of
the gutter on those letters rung by rung, and here he was at the top of
them, plainclothes, respectable, waving Harlan over with genuine
pleasure on his face.

\"Harl.\" He gripped his hand across the table. \"Sit. They do a mutton
thing here that\'s better than it has a right to be. How\'s Annis?\"

\"Keeping me honest.\"

\"Somebody has to. It\'s steady work.\" Aldean signalled for two of the
mutton thing without asking. \"Two years since you answered a letter of
mine, and now a note naming a chop-house. So either the shop\'s failed
or something\'s ripened. Which am I buying dinner for?\"

That was Aldean too. The kindness up front and the question inside it,
like a coin baked in a loaf.

\"The letter,\" Harlan said. \"Is it still good?\"

Aldean went still in a pleased, careful way, a man feeling a fish take
the hook he\'d left in the water two years ago and reminding himself not
to jerk the line.

\"That depends entirely,\" he said, \"on what you\'re carrying.\"

The food came. Harlan let it sit. He\'d walked here through the harbour
district with the whole of it arranged in his head, everything, names
and dates and the shape of the thing, a full confession with a price on
it, and now, sitting across from the mutton and the ink-stained finger
and the man who\'d asked after Annis, he found the arrangement had come
apart somewhere between the door and the chair.

\"Suppose a man knew of something coming,\" he said. \"Something large.
Suppose that man could put another man in your hands afterward, taken
with the goods on him, and the goods recovered whole, and the whole of
it loud enough to reach the broadsheets.\"

\"Suppose.\" Aldean cut his mutton. \"How large?\"

\"Large enough that the men above your chief would learn your name.\"

\"And the name of the man who brought it to you.\" Aldean chewed,
watching him. \"You said afterward. Why am I hearing about this after
and not before? Before, I could put men round it like a net. Before is
tidy.\"

\"Before, you\'d need to know things I\'m not telling you.\" Harlan kept
his voice at shop-counter level, pleasant, unhurried, feeling his heart
battering against his ribs. \"Who, where, what. And every one of those,
once said, is out of my hands and into a building full of clerks. No.
You get a man, and goods, and the morning to show up for.\"

Aldean set down his knife. The friendly reunion stepped aside, and the
investigator stood where it had been, and Harlan looked at that cold
appraising face and understood two things at once. The desk was real.
And it would not be given. It would be earned or refused, weighed out by
this man like anything else across a counter, and something in Harlan
settled at that, because a bought future is a debt, and an earned one is
his.

\"Let me teach you the trade you\'re asking to join,\" Aldean said. \"A
name ahead of time from a former criminal is nothing to me. Hearsay from
an unproven, untested informant. Informants don\'t get desks, Harl. They
get a copper coin and escorted through a back door.\" Aldean leaned
forward, the chop-house fading into the background. \"This isn\'t a
negotiation. It\'s an assessment. Prove that you want it. Give me
something. One question, and I\'ll know if you lie, because I've known
you since we were four. Who is he to you?\"

The chop-house went on around them. Spoons, low clerk voices, the
door\'s draught. Harlan looked at his old friend. He thought of the
painting sitting in the Pendleton vault, the dead partner who had bought
Harlan's life with his own silence, and the son who wore that same dead
man\'s smile.

*Give me something.*

He was the past, come calling to collect. He was everything the ribbon
tin and the patched tunic and the quiet leaning house had been built
against.

\"A customer,\" Harlan said.

It came out level. It came out perfect. Yet it felt like lifting green
timber with only your back, and in that moment he understood the
difference at last between lying to a watchman and lying to a man who
knew Annis\'s name.

\"A customer,\" Aldean repeated, in the voice of a man filing something
under a heading of his own choosing. \"Good. That\'s a start. But if you
want the desk, I need him in possession. Complete. Him, the goods, the
morning. You send me word when it ripens, a place and an hour, and I\'ll
have men enough to pick an apple without bruising the tree.\"

The door was twenty feet away. He measured it the old way, without
meaning to. Stand up now, plead the shop, shake the hand, and the letter
goes back in the drawer and softens for another two years, and in three
weeks a ship sails and none of this was ever anything. The whole future
sat there at the end of twenty feet of sawdust floor, available, cheap.

Then the investigator stepped back behind the friendliness, and he
raised his cup an inch. \"To trade, then. Send word when it ripens. And
Harl. Whatever it is you\'re not telling me.\" The cup paused. \"After
it\'s done, it\'s done clean. You\'ll be the man who came forward. Annis
never needs to hear it told any other way, and from where I sit,
there\'s no other way to tell it. You see that, don\'t you? Whatever
this weighs tonight, by summer it\'s the story of an honest man who did
his duty.\"

There it was. The whole desk, the whole future, wrapped and handed
across a chop-house table: a version of himself he could take home to
her and put on the shelf where folk could see. Legitimate. Aldean\'s old
word, and it had followed him like a stray for two years, and here he
was at last, putting down food for it.

\"I see it,\" Harlan said.

He walked home the long way, along the harbour, in the blue end of the
evening. Masts and rigging, the gulls going up the sky, the gate where a
bill hung fading in its frame of paste. He stopped once, outside a
boarding house on the harbour front with a card in the window, ROOMS BY
THE WEEK, and stood there a while in the manner of a man reading a card
in a window.

Then he went in and took the second-floor room, the one whose window
held the harbour gate the way a frame holds a picture, and paid the week
in advance, and told the landlady he was expecting a ship.

It wasn\'t a lie. He was fairly sure it wasn\'t a lie. On the walk home
he tried for a while to work out what it was instead, and gave it up
somewhere around Carver\'s Lane, and let himself in quietly, and stood
in the dark shop listening to the house settle, and did not look at the
bill in his own window, pale against the last of the light, facing the
lane like a good citizen.

## Part 3

### 1

The night before was the quietest Tav could remember.

They went over the plan one final time in the back room of the Buckled
Mast. Maddy marked the guard rotation with timestamps precise enough to
shame a clockmaker. Harlan reviewed the entry sequence in the level
voice of a man who had done this before and intended to do it well. Odd
Hands sat in the corner, still for once, listening.

Tav stood at the window and watched the street. It was quiet. His chest
wasn\'t.

\"Second bell after midnight,\" Maddy said.

\"Shift change at the second bell. Morning crew at the third. One bell.
We\'re in and out before they count the silver.\"

\"And the rotation is three weeks old.\" She didn\'t look up from her
timestamps. \"Sayd\'s man walked it three weeks ago, and tomorrow the
crown arrives. I\'d be happier if one thing in this plan were younger
than the bill on the harbour gate.\"

\"The grate was younger. I put my hands on it six days ago.\" Tav turned
from the window. \"A bank is a building full of habit, Maddy. That\'s
the whole trade of it. They sell habit to people who can afford it.
Rotations don\'t change in a bank because nothing changes in a bank.
That\'s what the sign over the door means.\"

\"The sweep was daylight,\" she said, and let it stand, and went back to
her timestamps, and the room absorbed it the way rooms absorbed the
things Maddy said once.

\"The ship sails at dawn,\" she said after a while. \"We\'re on it, or
we\'re here when the vault opens and someone comes to count the
silver.\"

\"We\'ll be on it with the next destination on the horizon.\"

\"Sounds like the perfect plan,\" Maddy said dryly.

\"Ah, a true believer. Like I keep telling you.\"

Harlan was packing the credentials satchel for the last time, though
there were no credentials left in it, only rope and wedges and the tools
of the night\'s trade. Methodical, precise. His hands were steady. His
face was steady. Everything about him was steady in a way that Tav found
reassuring and Maddy watched like a sailor watching a calm sky when she
could feel the storm in her bones.

She pulled Tav aside at the door after Harlan left.

\"Something\'s changed,\" she said.

\"You\'ve said that.\"

\"I\'m saying it again because it\'s more true now than it was then.\"

\"He went in rusty and came back sharp. That\'s what happens when you
put a good man back in his element.\"

\"That\'s not what I saw.\"

Tav put his hand on her shoulder. She didn\'t lean in and she didn\'t
step away. \"After tomorrow, it won\'t matter. We\'ll be on the water
and Harlan will go wherever Harlan goes, and none of this will follow
us.\"

She looked at his hand on her shoulder. Then at his face. Then she went
upstairs without saying goodnight, which was Maddy\'s way of telling him
she had heard him and disagreed and had decided not to waste words on
it.

He stood in the empty room and told himself she was wrong.

Sloppy, offered the voice, from somewhere it had no business being.

He ignored it. He was getting good at that.

---

He went upstairs and laid the night out on the bed. Dark work jacket,
soft boots, the knives, the wedges, cord. He dressed the plan piece by
piece the way other men dressed for church, and last of all he took the
folded pass from the lining of the travelling coat, heavy stock, the
seal readable by thumb in the dark, and moved it to the inner pocket of
the work jacket, over the heart, where the night\'s luck could ride with
him. The token belonged in the job. His father would have understood
that without being told.

Then he lay back with his boots on. The ring sat against his chest. The
pack sat by the door. The vault sat trusting at the top of Pendleton\'s
high street, and tomorrow it would be lighter, and the painting would be
in his hands, and he would take it to the safehouse and lay it beside
his father\'s things, and it would be done. The perfect heist.

He turned the ring through the fabric of his shirt and waited for
morning.

### 2

The second bell rang across Pendleton like a stone dropped in still
water.

Tav counted to thirty. Then he moved.

The lower access was where the sweep had promised: a service passage
built into the bank\'s foundation when the building was young and
security was a neglected notion. The grate sat in its wall of shadow,
and Tav put his hands on it and had the brief pleasure of being right.
Hinged left. Four bolts, and the two he\'d read as rust came away like
bad teeth, and the two he\'d read as bolts argued their case and lost.
Maddy took the grate\'s weight as it came free and set it against the
wall without a sound.

\"Forty counts ahead,\" she said.

\"Into the dark," Tav said and rubbed his hands together.

He went first. The passage was tight, old stone, the air damp and stale.
His shoulders brushed both walls. Behind him, Maddy and Odd Hands
followed.

They emerged into the vault\'s lower level: a storage chamber beneath
the main floor, used for overflow and the kind of items nobody checked
on twice.

Odd Hands pulled a shuttered lantern from his coat and flashed it twice
through the service grate toward the street. Somewhere above, Harlan
would be watching from the appointed position, ready to move to the main
entrance if anything changed. Ready to be the face, one last time.

The vault door between the lower and upper levels was the real lock. Tav
knelt in front of it and let his hands do the thinking. It was a good
lock. Better than the surveyor\'s, better than anything he\'d touched in
years. His fingers found the mechanism and started the conversation,
slow and patient, each pin a question that had one right answer and a
hundred wrong ones.

The bolt drew back. The door opened with a sound like a held breath
released.

\"Good lock,\" Tav said.

\"You sound like you\'re going to miss it,\" Maddy said.

\"I respect the craft.\"

They were in.

---

The vault in the dark was different from the vault in the lamplight.
Without the guards and the oil lamps and the comfortable fiction of a
routine inspection, it was just a room full of other people\'s things,
and Tav was there to make them his.

They worked fast. Maddy moved through the alcoves with a discipline Tav
admired and couldn\'t replicate. She knew what was worth carrying and
what wasn\'t, knew the weight-to-value ratio of everything they touched,
and she packed the take into canvas bags with the efficiency of someone
who had been leaving places in a hurry her whole life.

Tav went to the third alcove. West wall.

The Long View hung where it had hung for ten years, between the silver
and the deeds, waiting for nobody in particular.

He lifted it off the wall. It weighed almost nothing. A landscape, a
treeline, late light. He held it and felt the weight that wasn\'t in the
frame.

*That which has been abandoned is for someone new to claim.*

He wrapped it in cloth and put it in his coat, flat against his back,
and didn\'t say the words aloud, and didn\'t need to.

He was turning back to the alcoves when the building spoke.

It came through the stone before it came through the air: a deep seated
groan, iron and oak persuaded to move, two men\'s weight leaning into a
door built to want witnesses. The main vault door. The one that took two
men and announced itself like weather.

Somebody was opening the vault at half past the second bell.

Tav\'s lantern shutter was closed before the thought finished arriving.
Across the chamber Maddy\'s light died the same instant, and then her
hand found his shoulder in the black and pressed once: down. The door\'s
groan went on, unhurried, official, a sound with keys and salary behind
it, and lamplight began to leak down the entrance stair in a long yellow
seam.

They folded into the alcoves. Maddy took the deep gap behind the deed
boxes and pulled Odd Hands in after her by the collar, and Tav went flat
into the third alcove, into the space where a painting was no longer
hanging, bags dragged in against knees, and the dark stopped being
theirs.

Two lanterns came down the stair. Two men under them.

\"\...the whole of it, he says. Marshal\'s men crawling over everything
tomorrow, so tonight we walk it proper and sign the book.\" The first
voice was middle-aged and put upon. \"As if silver grows legs the one
night somebody watches.\"

\"Procession eve.\" The second voice was younger and didn\'t care.
\"Everything\'s proper on Procession eve. Even us.\"

Boots on the vault floor. Lamplight swung along the alcoves in slow
arcs, and Tav stood in the gap on the west wall with a stolen painting
against his spine and roughly four inches of shadow doing the work of a
wall. He breathed through his nose, into his collar, the way you breathe
when breathing is a decision. Sayd\'s rotation had the vault sealed from
second bell to third. Sayd\'s rotation had been right, once, about a
world that no longer existed, because somewhere above them a head guard
had woken up yesterday with the Procession on his neck and improved
things.

Which of his facts is the next bad night. Maddy\'s voice, weeks old,
sitting in the dark with him like a fourth thief.

The lanterns worked closer. The older guard walked the east wall,
trailing light across silver and strongboxes, bored in the specific way
of a man performing thoroughness for a logbook. The younger came down
the west side.

He stopped at the third alcove.

The light came into the gap up to Tav\'s boots and sat there. Above him,
past the edge of the alcove\'s shadow, the wall carried a picture hook
and a pale rectangle of un-dusted stone where ten years of vault dark
had shaded around a frame, and the frame was inside Tav\'s coat, and Tav
stood in the black four inches from the light and did not exist. He
thought about nothing. Thinking was a luxury for people who weren\'t
pressed against cold stone wearing the evidence.

\"Here,\" the younger guard said. \"Was there always a hook there?\"

A pause the length of a small ice age.

\"There\'s hooks everywhere, half of them empty. Deposits come and go.\"
The older voice, farther off, entirely uninterested. \"You going to
catalogue the walls, or are we signing the book before third bell?
Because I\'m not explaining to the day men why the round ran long.\"

The light hung in the gap one count more. Two.

\"Suppose,\" the younger one said, and the light slid off, and the boots
moved on.

They finished the round the way bored men finish rounds, faster at the
end than the start. The lanterns climbed the stair, the great door
groaned back into its frame, keys worked, and the dark came home to the
vault like a tide.

Nobody moved for a ten count. Then Maddy\'s shutter opened a finger\'s
width, one thin blade of light, and Tav peeled himself out of the
alcove, and as he came free of the gap something whispered down the
stone behind him, paper-soft, and landed in the dust where the light
didn\'t reach, and the vault kept it, and Tav, whose heart was
performing an entirely separate burglary inside his ribs, heard nothing
at all.

\"The book,\" Maddy breathed. \"They sign a book. Which means someone
reads the book. Which means the round happens again, or it doesn\'t, and
we don\'t know which.\"

\"Then we\'re done being greedy.\" Tav looked once around the chamber,
at the alcoves they\'d stripped and the ones they\'d never touch now,
and let them go. \"Bags. We\'re out.\"

### 3

Odd Hands was watching the passage. \"Quarter bell,\" he said, with the
particular calm of someone young enough to think a quarter bell was a
lot.

\"Then we don't spend it," Tav said.

They moved back through the lower level. Maddy first, bags distributed
between them, fast and quiet. The passage was tighter going out with the
take, and Tav\'s coat caught on the stone where the painting pressed
flat against his back. He freed it without stopping.

Halfway through, Odd Hands said, \"Wait.\"

Tav stopped. Maddy stopped. The dark was complete.

\"Footsteps,\" Odd Hands whispered. \"Above.\"

They listened. Tav heard nothing for five counts, then: boots on stone.
Measured, steady, passing overhead. Not the vault men. The town watch,
out walking Procession eve like everyone else in Pendleton with a
uniform and orders to be seen in it. The whole night had extra men in
it, that was the lesson, written everywhere at once.

The boots stopped. Directly above them.

Tav pressed flat against the wall and breathed through his nose and
didn\'t move and didn\'t think about the painting between his shoulder
blades or the grate at the end of the passage or the ship waiting in the
harbour. He thought about nothing. Thinking was a luxury for people who
weren\'t pressed against cold stone beneath a bank they\'d just robbed.

The boots moved on.

They waited thirty counts. Then Maddy exhaled, which was as close to
panic as Maddy came, and they moved.

The grate. The street. Cold air on his face. Odd Hands replaced the
grate and they walked through the back streets of Pendleton in the small
hours: three people carrying canvas bags and looking like exactly
nothing worth remembering.

Harlan met them at the crossroads below the high street. He fell into
step without a word.

\"Clean?\" he asked.

\"Clean." Tav was grinning. He couldn't help it.

The horses were where they\'d left them, saddled and patient behind the
inn. They rode hard through the dark, four riders on the Merranth road,
bags heavy across the saddles. Nobody spoke. The moon sat low and the
road was empty and the vault behind them was lighter by several hundred
pounds of silver, gold, and one painting, and somewhere behind them two
bored men had signed a book that said all was well.

Tav wanted to laugh. He swallowed it. Professionals don\'t laugh until
they\'re on the water and below deck.

They reached Merranth before the fourth bell.

They dismounted in the shadow of the harbour wall, and the harbour
breathed beyond it, rigging and tide-slap and the first grey working
into the east. The ship was out there. He could feel it the way you feel
a held door.

\"We split here,\" Maddy said. \"Bags go aboard with me and the boy. You
two make your own ways to the dock. Small groups, clean hands.\"

Tav nodded. \"I have something to do first.\"

She had half turned toward the harbour, and she stopped, and came back
around all the way, which he should have read for what it was.

\"No,\" she said. \"You don\'t.\"

\"Maddy.\"

\"The tide turns at dawn. That\'s not a bell from now. Whatever it is,
it has waited five years. It can wait for open water.\" Her voice stayed
level and quiet and her eyes had gone the flat calm they went in the
worst moments of jobs. \"There is a bill with your coat on it nailed to
that gate. There were extra men in Pendleton tonight and there will be
extra men everywhere by noon, and the one place in this city with your
name already written on the wall is the ground between here and wherever
you think you\'re going.\"

\"It\'s an hour. Both ways, with time to spare.\"

\"Then it\'s an hour you\'re spending on something you won\'t name to
me.\" She stepped in. Close enough that it was nobody else\'s
conversation, though Harlan stood three horses away and the boy was a
shadow by the wall. \"I don\'t ask, Tav. You know I don\'t ask. It\'s
the first rule I ever kept and I have kept it my whole life, and I\'m
breaking it standing in front of you. I\'m asking. Come to the ship
now.\"

And there it was, the whole of it, offered across eight inches of cold
harbour air. The thing she never did, done. He looked at her and he
could feel the shape of telling her, the safehouse, the workbench, his
father\'s things and his own fourteen small answers to a dead man, all
of it right there behind his teeth, and what came out instead was light,
because that was how he said the things that mattered most, and because
the plan was perfect, and the night had proved it, and in one hour there
would be a lifetime of telling her everything on the water.

\"I\'ll be at the dock before the tide turns.\"

Maddy looked at him for a long time. Something went over her face like
wind over water, there and gone, and afterward the calm was different.
Older.

\"Where?\"

\"Somewhere I need to go.\" A grin, hung carefully. \"Trust me.\"

\"I don\'t repeat myself,\" she said. \"You\'ve heard me say that. So
hear this the once. If you are not on that dock when the tide turns, I
will not be standing on it wondering why.\"

She shouldered her pack and took the bags and went, striding down toward
the water the way she moved through every room she\'d already decided to
leave, and Odd Hands trailed after her with the boy\'s portion of the
load, glancing back once.

Harlan lingered.

\"You should go with them,\" Tav said.

\"I\'ll make my own way.\" Harlan put out his hand. \"Good work in
there.\"

Tav took the hand. Warm and large and steady. \"My father would have
been proud of you.\"

\"Get to the ship, Tav.\"

Harlan walked away into the dark, in the wrong direction for the
harbour. His hand found the key in his coat pocket, the one for the room
he\'d rented three days ago, second floor of a boarding house with a
clear sightline to the harbour gate. He\'d told the landlady he was
expecting a ship.

Tav watched him go and thought nothing of it because he was already
thinking about the safehouse.

### 4

The safehouse was where his father had left it, which was the only thing
his father had left that nobody had taken, sold, or filed in a vault for
ten years.

A single room above a disused tannery on the east side of the Warrens,
accessed by a staircase that objected to visitors and a door with a lock
his father had installed himself. Tav pulled the key from the lining of
his hat, where it had sat sewn into the band for five years. He hadn\'t
used it since the day he\'d left Merranth. The lock turned.

The room was dust and dark and the smell of leather that had seeped into
everything from the tannery below. Tav lit the lamp. Shadows pulled back
from the corners. Everything was where it had been: the workbench, the
tools, the narrow bed. A man\'s things, arranged by a man who expected
to come home.

He set the pack on the workbench and took out the trinkets. One by one.
Fourteen objects in a row. The copper mask, the carved crow, the brass
disc, the wooden sailor, the rest. Twenty-six ports over five years.
Things that fixed a moment in place and said *I was here*.

He unwrapped the painting and set it against the wall beneath the lamp.

The Long View. Hills, a valley, a treeline catching late light. His
father\'s last score. His father\'s dying act, propped against the wall
of a room that smelled of leather and dust.

*I brought them home, Father. All of them.*

He sat on the edge of the narrow bed and looked at the painting and let
the room be what it was. A shrine. A beacon. A place where a boy had
become a thief because his father had been one.

He didn\'t cry. He sat for exactly as long as it took to feel what
needed feeling, and then he was going to walk to the harbour and get on
a ship and sail to the Southern Reach with a girl who was smarter than
him, and start something new.

*Ten breaths,* he told himself.

He took twenty.

---

It was somewhere past fifty that he noticed the treeline.

He\'d noticed it in the vault, too. The way the trees broke along the
ridge. He\'d written it off as the painter\'s style. But sitting here in
the lamplight, closer than he\'d ever been, the pattern resolved.

It wasn\'t trees.

It was a road. The treeline traced a road, or the memory of one. The
gaps between the trunks too regular to be natural, the angles too
deliberate. And once he saw the road, he saw the rest: the valley
oriented around a compass bearing, the ridge marking elevation, the
light fixing direction. The whole landscape was a set of coordinates,
painted in the language of hills and trees and afternoon sun.

Not a landscape. A map.

He stared at it. Then he closed his eyes and held the image, every line,
every tree, the exact angle of the ridge and the fall of the light, and
he committed it to memory the way he committed locks: by feel, by
structure, by the patience to let it speak for itself.

When he opened his eyes, he knew where it pointed. Not what was there. A
house, a ruin, an empty field. The address solidified in his mind.

*The whole world is in the frame.*

He\'d always thought it was a saying.

He stood. Wrapped the painting and put it back in his coat. He looked at
the trinkets on the workbench, fourteen objects in a row, marveling at
how they had found their way home. But what would become of this place?
Would he come back?

*That which has been abandoned is for someone new to claim.*

He left the safehouse as it was.

---

Outside, in the alley below the tannery, a slight figure pressed flat
against the wall and watched the lamplight go out.

Odd Hands waited until the footsteps faded. Then he turned and ran,
quiet and quick, toward Lowertown.

### 5

The harbour was grey and cold and the tide was coming in and the ship
was there, masts against the early sky, and Tav walked toward it with
the painting flat against his back and the address in his head and the
certainty that the world was, for once, exactly the shape he\'d wanted
it.

He\'d put the coat back on at the safehouse. The travelling coat, the
real one, blue at the collar, retired these three weeks by solemn
agreement. Until we sail, he\'d promised, and they were sailing, and a
man should be dressed as himself on the morning his life comes right.
He\'d stood in his father\'s room and shrugged it on over the wrapped
painting like the last piece of the plan.

He saw Maddy first. On the dock, pack at her feet, arms folded,
watching. He raised a hand.

She didn\'t wave back.

Something about her posture. The weight too far forward, the arms folded
too tight. He was fifty yards from the dock when it arrived, the
wrongness, settling over him like the cold in the cellar.

\"Tav Derring.\"

The voice came from behind and to his left. He didn\'t recognize it.

\"By authority of Special Investigations, you are under arrest for theft
from the Pendleton Repository. Set down what you\'re carrying and place
your hands where we can see them.\"

Four of them. Three in uniform, one in plainclothes. They\'d been
waiting in the lee of a warehouse, positioned so the dock was behind
them and the street was behind Tav, and there was nowhere that wasn\'t
through somebody. The plainclothes man\'s eyes went once to Tav\'s
collar, to the blue showing at the lapel, and came back with the settled
look of a man matching a bill he\'d read a hundred times, and Tav
understood, in the small distinct way you understand the individual
stones of an avalanche, that he had dressed for them.

Sloppy, was all the voice said.

Tav stood very still.

He thought about the service passage. He thought about the rooftops
above the warehouses and the drainpipe on the chandler\'s that could
hold his weight. He thought about the painting on his back and the ship
fifty yards away and Maddy standing on the dock watching this happen.

He thought about running. The painting was delicate. He couldn\'t run at
speed with it on his back; he\'d have to leave it behind. Resigned, and
out of options, he took it from his coat and set it down on the cobbles.
The way you set down something that has waited ten years to come home.

\"Hands,\" the plainclothes officer said.

They searched him. Thorough and impersonal. They found the necklace
under his shirt. The ring. He felt the silver leave his skin and felt
something leave with it.

\"There\'s been a mistake,\" he said, because saying something was
better than silence, even when it wasn\'t true. \"My name is Vic. I\'m
from Sudlow.\"

\"Your name is Tavish Derring,\" the officer said, \"and you were named
this morning by a reliable informant with direct knowledge of the
operation.\"

Named.

This morning.

A reliable informant with direct knowledge.

He looked at the dock. Maddy was gone. Her pack was gone. The space
where she\'d been standing was empty, and whether she\'d run or walked
or simply done what Maddy always did when things went wrong, the result
was the same.

They put irons on his wrists. The metal was cold and sat against the
skin where Sayd\'s rope had sat all those weeks ago, at the start of it,
when the plan was still perfect. He laughed, but the mirth didn\'t
extend beyond the escaping sound.

They walked him back through the harbour, past the warehouses and the
early-morning men who looked up from their work and looked down again
because an arrest was none of their business. The ship sat at the dock
behind him, and the tide was coming, and the Southern Reach was a
direction he would not be sailing.

---

On the second floor of a boarding house across from the harbour gate,
behind a dirt-stained and cracked window, Harlan watched.

The note had gone at first light, by the landlady\'s boy, a shilling for
the running and no answer required: This morning. The gate, before the
tide. Tall, lean, dark travelling coat, blue at the collar. He will be
carrying a wrapped board, flat, the length of an arm. He had written it
at the washstand by candle-end, in a plain clear hand, and read it once,
and it had read like a bill of lading, and he had sent it before it
could read like anything else.

One name. One description. Nothing about a woman from Dolliver who would
be standing on the dock, nothing about a boy who could vanish into a
crowd of two. Aldean had asked for a man in possession, and a man in
possession was what the note contained, complete, and not one soul more.
Harlan had gone over it in the night the way he\'d once gone over locks,
and it held. It was, he had decided somewhere toward dawn, the only
mercy the arrangement had room for, and he was keeping it like a man
keeps the last of his money.

He watched them put the irons on. He watched them take the painting. He
watched them walk Tav back through the street, and he watched Tav not
look back at the ship, and he watched until there was nothing left to
watch and the harbour was just a harbour again.

His hands were steady. He looked down at them resting on the windowsill.
Three weeks ago, tracing the blueprints of the Pendleton vault, they had
felt alive for the first time in a decade. Now, they were just a
shopkeeper\'s hands again, still and heavy and perfectly suited for
sorting inventory that didn\'t need sorting.

He left the window, and he went downstairs.

He walked to the offices of Special Investigations on the south side of
the harbour district and presented himself at the front desk and said,
\"My name is Harlan. I believe Aldean Blundo is expecting me.\"

The desk clerk went to check.

Harlan waited.

### 6

The safehouse was a single room above a disused tannery on the east side
of the Warrens, and Sayd stood in the middle of it and looked at what
Tav had left behind.

The fourteen objects on a workbench. The copper mask, carved stone bird,
the brass disc, and others. Trinkets. Souvenirs. The collection of a
sentimental, naive fool who didn't think things through. Sayd looked at
them and understood what they were and felt nothing about them and moved
on.

The room had been searched already, by his own people, in the hour
between Odd Hands\' report and his arrival. They\'d found what he\'d
sent them to find. Not on the workbench. Not with the trinkets. Beneath
the floorboards, in a tin box sealed with wax, exactly where a boy of
fifteen would think to hide the most dangerous thing he\'d ever stolen.

The Accord\'s political ledger.

Sayd opened it at the workbench, beside the trinkets, and read.

Names. Dates. Transactions. The careful bookkeeping of an organization
that had been running longer than anyone alive could remember, laid out
by a clerk who understood that records outlast their keepers. Every
debt, every payment, every arrangement between the Accord and the men
who thought they governed Merranth. The Patrons. The harbourmasters. The
magistrates. The men behind the men, all of them named and dated and
quantified.

This was how Tav had done it. Not cunning. Not courage. Leverage. A boy
of fifteen had stolen the most comprehensive account of the Accord\'s
dealings and said *release me or I release this*, and the Accord, which
had never released anyone, had calculated the cost and chosen the
cheaper option.

Simple. The kind of move that only works once, and only works for
someone too young to understand how lucky he\'d been.

Sayd closed the book. He looked at the trinkets on the workbench,
abandoned once again by a man who intended to come back for them.

*That which has been abandoned is for someone new to claim.*

He could hear himself saying it. A month ago, in a cellar, to a man in a
chair. He\'d meant it about trinkets. He meant it now about everything.

He took the ledger and left the trinkets where they were.

Downstairs, Halst was waiting by the door. He fell into step without a
word.

\"The boy?\"

\"Sent him home.\"

\"Good.\"

They walked through the Warrens. Merranth was waking up, doing its usual
poor impression of a city worth living in. Sayd held the ledger under
his arm and thought about architecture. Not the buildings. The structure
of a machine that had been running for generations, collecting debts
that served a purpose nobody remembered, feeding something it had
forgotten how to name.

He was going to use it. Not to escape the Accord, as Tav had. To own it.

He reached for the tabac and didn\'t light it. He had a long morning
ahead. But not an unpleasant one.

### 7

The cell was small and clean and didn\'t have an opinion about him,
which was more than most rooms had managed lately.

Tav sat on the stone bench with his back against the wall and looked at
his hands. They\'d taken the ring. The painting. The necklace. The coat,
even, folded over a clerk\'s arm like laundry, blue collar showing, off
to wherever they filed the things a man used to be. He\'d walked in with
his father\'s legacy on his back and walked out with exactly what he\'d
started with: nothing, and a tendency to antagonize authority figures.

Named this morning by a reliable informant with direct knowledge.

He knew who. He\'d known since the officer said it. Maybe before. Maybe
since the moment in the shop, when Harlan\'s shoulders dropped and his
hands came off the counter and the man behind the counter came alive in
a way the shop never allowed. Maybe since his father died and Harlan
lived, and the debt between them had been running in the wrong direction
ever since.

He\'d believed. He\'d always been good at believing things.

Somewhere in the inventory of his pockets, cataloguing what they\'d
taken the way he\'d checked the trinkets a hundred times, by touch, by
absence, his mind reached the inner pocket of the work jacket and
stopped.

They hadn\'t taken the pass. He would have felt it go, the way he\'d
felt the ring go. He reached for the memory of it instead and worked
backward through the night, hand over hand, the safehouse, the ride, the
passage, the vault, and arrived at the third alcove on the west wall, at
four inches of shadow and a lantern sitting against his boots, at the
moment he\'d peeled himself out of the gap with his heart going like a
drum crew.

Paper-soft. Something had gone down the stone behind him, and the dark
had kept it, and he\'d heard it and filed it nowhere, because he was
busy not existing.

The pass was in the vault. Heavy stock, Procession seal, a false name in
a clerk\'s hand, lying in the dust of the third alcove under an empty
hook, waiting for the day men and their logbook. The perfect heist\'s
perfect token. His father had gone to his death rather than hand over a
name, and his son had carried one into the vault on good paper, for
sentiment, and left it there like a signed confession. Things generally
kept, if you kept them carefully. He had kept it so carefully it would
hang him.

He laughed. It came out wrong in the small room, and he stopped.

His father had collected things that said I was here, and this happened,
and it mattered. Well. It had said all three.

Sloppy, said the voice, for the last time, and it was right, and he was
tired, and the word settled like a stone he\'d swallowed the morning he
stepped off the August Celeste and never quite coughed up.

He closed his eyes. The cell was quiet. The harbour was out there
somewhere, and the ship was gone, and Maddy was gone, and the painting
was in an evidence room wearing a tag with his real name on it. But the
treeline was still behind his eyes. The ridge, the valley, the angle of
the light, the road hidden in the trees. The address was in his head,
committed to memory, and that was one lock no one could crack.

They could take everything he\'d carried. They couldn\'t take what he\'d
seen.

He sat in the quiet for a long time. And once, in the silence between
one breath and the next, he could have sworn he heard the ring. Not
silver on stone. Something beneath the metal, a faint clear note that
had no source and lasted exactly as long as a heartbeat.

Exhaustion, he decided.

He leaned his head against the wall and closed his eyes and waited for
whatever came next.

---

She hadn\'t run.

She\'d wanted to. Every rule Madame Wove had carved into her bones,
every lesson from every port and every job gone wrong, said the same
thing: go. The ship is right there. The tide is coming. He is not your
problem.

She\'d watched them put the irons on from behind a stack of cargo crates
at the end of the dock. She\'d watched them take the painting from his
hands. She\'d watched his face do the thing it did when the performance
ran out and there was nothing underneath except the person, and the
person was afraid.

And there was still a door. The second berth, the later tide, the
different dock, a name that had never been spoken above a table in the
back room of the Buckled Mast. Harlan\'s door behind the door, arranged
by the one of them who thought of everything, and she stood behind the
crates with her pack on her shoulder and the whole of the Southern Reach
on the far side of it, hers, paid for, open. Nobody was watching the
second dock. She had checked, because she was Maddy, and checking was
the second rule she\'d ever kept.

She thought about the Southern Reach. The islands. The ruins she\'d been
planning to visit since before him. The one place that was hers, the one
she\'d found on her own, the one she\'d been ready to sail for whether
he came or not.

She thought about the ring he still carried. The one she\'d stolen from
a market stall in a port town two years ago and handed to him like it
was nothing. The one he\'d never taken off.

She thought about what kind of person leaves when the only person who
ever made her stop leaving needs her not to.

She didn\'t board the ship. Either ship.

---

The walk to Lowertown was long enough for her to change her mind three
times. She didn\'t.

She knew where Sayd\'s office was because she paid attention, which was
apparently a rare skill in the company she kept.

The building was unremarkable. Stairs, a corridor, a door. She knocked.

\"Come in,\" said a voice that expected someone else.

She opened the door. Sayd was behind his desk, pipe unlit, a
leather-bound book she didn\'t recognise open in front of him. A large
Palor man stood by the window, vast and still. The room smelled of
ginger.

Sayd looked at her the way a man looks at an unexpected piece on the
board.

\"Tav\'s been arrested,\" she said.

\"I know who you are. And I know.\" He set the pipe down. \"The question
is why you\'re here instead of on a ship.\"

\"I want him back.\"

Sayd closed the book. He set his hands flat on the desk and chose to be
interested.

\"And what are you offering?\"

She sat down without being asked. Under her ribs, packed as carefully as
anything she owned, was the one thing she had that was worth what she
was buying.

\"A city that hasn\'t been found yet.\"
